## CHAPTER 1: THE TESTOSTERONE MYTH

It's Tuesday morning, and you're sitting in your GP's surgery, feeling like you're about to admit something shameful.

Your name's Mark. You're 42, you run a small consultancy, you go to the gym three times a week, and for the last two years you've felt like someone's slowly turned down the volume on your life. You wake up tired. Sex feels like a chore you used to enjoy. Your mates at work are talking about promotions and you can barely summon the energy for the meeting you're running. You've got a bit more belly despite training the same way. You think it might be testosterone, because you've seen the ads on Instagram and heard the lads talk about it in the gym, but you also know it sounds ridiculous—testosterone boosters are what idiot football fans sell in sketchy supplements, right?

Your GP—nice person, busy, probably sees eighty patients a day—does a quick physical, asks how you're sleeping (fine, more or less), whether you're under stress (aren't we all?), and suggests you're probably just overworked. He checks a few things in the computer, says your testosterone is "normal," and offers you an SSRI.

You don't take it. Instead, you do what anyone does in 2026: you Google "testosterone deficiency men," fall down a YouTube rabbit hole of people selling £50 bottles of tribulus, read some forum posts that sound like aliens discussing human reproduction, and close the laptop feeling confused and slightly annoyed at yourself for wasting the time.

You're not alone. This is the experience of thousands of British men every year.

The problem isn't your GP. She's working within a system that treats testosterone like a specialist topic and most men like they're overreacting. The problem also isn't the supplement industry, though they're certainly not helping. The real problem is that testosterone has become so laden with mythology—both the "it'll fix everything" gym-bro version and the "it's not a real problem" medical dismissal version—that men like you are left standing in the middle with no useful information.

This book exists because that gap is massive, and it's making British men unnecessarily tired and unmotivated.

### The Cultural Problem

We've somehow managed to create a situation where testosterone is either a taboo subject that doctors dismiss or an obsession of people trying to sell you something. There's no middle ground.

On one side, you've got the gym culture. Testosterone becomes shorthand for "getting jacked," and anyone talking about it is assumed to be a meathead who wants to get huge or is using steroids. On the other side, there's the medical establishment version. If you're at 380 ng/dL and you feel terrible, the message is: that's normal, stop worrying, see a therapist. The implicit message: if you're interested in testosterone and you're not in the bottom 1%, you're a hypochondriac.

The truth—and this is where I'm going to keep coming back—is that you can be within the "normal range" and still be experiencing real decline relative to your own baseline, and that's worth taking seriously.

### What Testosterone Actually Is (The Five-Minute Version)

You need to understand what you're dealing with before we talk about whether it matters.

Testosterone is a steroid hormone. That word "steroid" is scary to most people because we associate it with cheating athletes, but a steroid is just a type of hormone with a particular chemical structure. Your body produces lots of steroids naturally. Testosterone is one. It's made primarily in the testes (the Leydig cells, specifically), with a smaller amount made in the adrenal glands on top of your kidneys.

It's controlled by your pituitary gland, which sits at the base of your brain and gets its instructions from a part called the hypothalamus. This system—hypothalamus, pituitary, gonads—is called the HPG axis, and we'll talk about it properly in Chapter 2. For now: your brain tells your pituitary to make LH (luteinizing hormone), LH tells your testes to make testosterone, and when testosterone levels get high enough, negative feedback tells your brain to chill out. It's like a thermostat.

Once testosterone is made, it travels in your bloodstream. But it doesn't travel alone. Most of it (about 97%) gets picked up by binding proteins—mainly one called SHBG (sex hormone-binding globulin). Only about 2-3% floats around freely, unbound. This free testosterone is what's actually available to affect your tissues. Everything else is just along for the ride.

What does testosterone do? This is important because people oversimplify it.

It builds and maintains muscle tissue. It increases protein synthesis and makes your muscles more responsive to resistance training. It also helps you recover between training sessions. But it doesn't build muscle on its own—you still need stimulus (lifting) and calories and protein. Testosterone without training doesn't make you muscular; training with low testosterone makes building muscle harder.

It affects bone density. Low testosterone over time leads to weaker bones. That's not glamorous, but it matters.

It plays a role in sexual function. Erections, libido, sexual motivation—testosterone influences all of these, though it's not the whole story. Low testosterone can lead to erectile dysfunction, but so can blood pressure medications, anxiety, poor sleep, and relationship problems.

It influences mood and motivation. This is harder to study, but the evidence is clear: people with low testosterone report lower motivation, more fatigue, and higher rates of depression. But—and this is crucial—correlation isn't causation. Does low testosterone cause depression, or does depression lower testosterone? The answer is probably "both, and it's complicated."

It affects body composition. Testosterone encourages your body to preferentially store fat in the subcutaneous layer (under the skin) rather than visceral layer (around organs), and it helps preserve muscle mass when you're in a calorie deficit. Low testosterone often comes with easier fat gain and harder muscle retention.

It influences the timing and intensity of all these things. Your testosterone isn't static. It peaks in the early morning (roughly 6-8am for most men) and drops throughout the day. It's seasonal (slightly higher in autumn and winter). It changes across your lifespan—it's highest in your twenties and gradually declines about 1% per year after about 30, with more variance between individual men than that average suggests.

Here's what testosterone doesn't do: it doesn't make you confident. It doesn't make you intelligent. It doesn't make you successful. It doesn't make you a better person. It's a hormone that influences some biological processes that are related to mood and motivation, but the relationship is complex and modulated by everything else in your life. A man with low testosterone who sleeps well, trains hard, has good relationships, and feels purposeful will often feel better than a man with high testosterone who's depressed, sleep-deprived, and stuck in a job he hates.

This distinction matters because the supplement industry sells testosterone as a life-changing miracle. It's not. It's foundational. If it's low, getting it into a good range can make a real difference. But it's not the only lever, and raising it won't fix problems that aren't actually testosterone-related.

### The "Normal Range" Problem

Here's where things get interesting and where most men get let down by the system.

When your GP tests your testosterone, they get a number and a reference range. The reference range is typically something like "10.4-40.9 nmol/L" (in UK units) or "300-900 ng/dL" (in American units). The range is the entire span of values seen in a representative population of healthy adult men. If you're in that range, you're officially "normal."

But here's the problem: a reference range is a statistical concept, not a clinical concept. It tells you where the population is; it doesn't tell you where you need to be to feel well.

Imagine a reference range for pain tolerance. You test it on 1,000 healthy men and you get a range from 3 to 9 out of 10. Someone at 3? They're "normal." Someone at 9? Also "normal." But they're going to have a completely different lived experience.

Testosterone is the same. A man at 300 ng/dL and a man at 900 ng/dL are both "normal," but they're going to feel wildly different. The man at 900 will have more energy, better recovery, easier muscle gain, better libido. The man at 300 will feel like someone turned down the volume on his life. Both are within the normal range.

The other problem is that normal ranges are population-based, not individual-based. Your individual baseline matters. If you spent your twenties and thirties naturally at 650 ng/dL, and you're now at 380 ng/dL, you've declined by 40%. You're "normal." You're also experiencing significant change relative to where you started.

This is why your GP's test result—which is often just total testosterone—can be almost useless without context. You need to know your free testosterone, your SHBG, your baseline if you have one. You need to know whether you've declined from where you personally used to be.

And you need a doctor who understands this distinction, which is rarer than it should be.

### What Low Testosterone Genuinely Causes

Let's be clear about what the evidence shows.

Low testosterone is associated with fatigue. The relationship is dose-dependent: the lower your testosterone, the more fatigue you're likely to experience. But it's not a one-to-one relationship, and not every fatigued man has low testosterone. If you're exhausted, your testosterone could be fine and you might have sleep apnea, depression, thyroid problems, or just be overworked.

Low testosterone is associated with reduced libido and erectile dysfunction. Again, dose-dependent, not absolute. Low testosterone is definitely a common cause of sexual dysfunction in middle-aged men. It's also not the only cause. Anxiety, relationship problems, poor blood flow, and other hormonal issues can all cause the same symptoms.

Low testosterone is associated with mood changes. Men with low testosterone report more irritability, more anxiety, lower motivation, and higher rates of depression. The evidence is solid. But again: is the low testosterone causing the mood changes, or are the mood changes causing the low testosterone (because chronic stress suppresses testosterone)? Almost certainly both.

Low testosterone is associated with easier fat gain and harder muscle retention. This is well-established. If your testosterone is low, your body is more willing to store calories as fat and more likely to break down muscle when in a deficit. It's not impossible to stay lean with low testosterone, but it's harder.

Low testosterone is associated with weaker bones. Osteoporosis in men is often under-diagnosed, but low testosterone is a major risk factor. Over decades, this matters.

What's important is the word "associated." We can't say that low testosterone causes all of these things. We can say: if you have low testosterone plus symptoms, raising it often makes the symptoms better. Whether it would have made them better without the testosterone part is sometimes unclear.

This is why testing matters. If you feel terrible and you test and you have low testosterone, raising it is a reasonable experiment. If you feel terrible and you test and your testosterone is fine, blaming testosterone is probably wrong, and you need to investigate other causes.

### Why Everyone Gets It Wrong

The gym industry gets it wrong because they sell it as a magic fix. "Boost your testosterone and you'll gain 10kg of muscle, lose your belly, and feel like a superhero." This is marketing. Yes, testosterone matters for muscle gain. Yes, raising low testosterone from 300 to 700 ng/dL will make a real difference. But it's not magic. Most of muscle gain comes from training and eating enough. Most of fat loss comes from managing calories. Testosterone is an important variable, not the only variable.

The medical establishment often gets it wrong because they treat it as a binary: either you have a disease (hypogonadism) requiring treatment, or you don't, and if you don't, you're fine. This ignores the spectrum. A man at 380 ng/dL might be objectively experiencing decline and genuinely benefit from treatment, even if he's above the threshold for an NHS diagnosis of hypogonadism.

The supplement industry gets it wrong because they're selling hope. Tribulus, fenugreek, tongkat ali, ZMA, D-aspartic acid—they've created an entire product category around "natural testosterone boosters." Some of these have research. Most of it isn't great. But they're incredibly profitable because men want the problem fixed cheaply and easily, and "take this supplement" is way easier to implement than "sleep better, train consistently, eat properly, manage your stress for six months."

The fitness influencer culture gets it wrong because they treat testosterone as a proxy for health and masculinity, which it isn't. A man with low testosterone who's thoughtful, present, and healthy is healthier than a man with high testosterone who's sleep-deprived, stressed, and alone.

Media and cultural commentary get it wrong by either treating testosterone like it's everything or treating it like it's nothing. Neither is accurate.

### Why Most Men Don't Know Their Hormone Numbers

The NHS doesn't routinely test hormones. If you go to your GP and say, "I'd like my testosterone tested," they'll test it if you ask directly, but they won't test the full picture. They'll test total testosterone. If it's above 8 nmol/L (roughly 230 ng/dL), they'll say it's normal and send you away. If it's below that, congratulations, you can potentially get TRT. There's no middle zone where they say, "Your testosterone is in the normal range, but it's still low for you, let's investigate further."

They won't test free testosterone unless you specifically ask, and many GPs will resist because they think it's not reliable (it's not the most reliable test, but the alternatives aren't better). They won't test SHBG, oestradiol, LH, FSH, or prolactin unless there's a specific reason. They definitely won't test them together as a system.

This makes sense from an NHS efficiency standpoint. Testing costs money. The system is overburdened. Most men who walk in don't actually have endocrine disorders; they're just overtired. But it also means that if you have a real problem that doesn't fit into the binary "yes hypogonadism / no hypogonadism," you'll be missed.

The other reason most men don't know their hormone numbers is awareness. Testosterone testing isn't marketed to regular men. It's marketed to gym guys and guys in supplement ads. A busy professional doesn't expect to walk into his GP's surgery and get a full hormone panel. It feels like something for specialists or people who are desperate. So they don't ask.

And the medical conservatism doesn't help. The message from a lot of the system is: if you're not in crisis, don't worry about it. Manage your stress, sleep better, exercise more. Come back in six months if you're still tired. This advice isn't wrong—stress, sleep, and exercise do matter enormously—but it can lead to years of unnecessarily poor health if those things aren't actually the problem.

So: you need to either push your GP to test the full panel, or you go private. Both are options. We'll cover both.

### The Ten Essential Markers

Let's say you're getting tested. What exactly are you measuring, and why?

There are about fifteen different hormone markers that could matter. But for most men doing this, you're looking at a core ten. Here's what they are, what they mean, what optimal looks like, and what it means if they're off.

**1. Total Testosterone**

What it measures: The sum of all testosterone in your blood—bound to SHBG, bound to albumin, and free.

What it does: Reflects your overall testosterone production.

Optimal range: 500-700 ng/dL (17-24 nmol/L). This is where most men feel good. The NHS range is typically 8-28 nmol/L, which is enormous and clinically useless.

Low means: Below 350 ng/dL (12 nmol/L), most men will experience fatigue, reduced libido, worse recovery. This is treatable.

High means: Above 900 ng/dL (31 nmol/L), you're at higher risk for cardiovascular issues, higher haematocrit, mood changes, possible acne. Needs monitoring.

Measurement reliability: Good. This is the standard test. Simple. Reliable. However, it only tells you half the story.

**2. Free Testosterone**

What it measures: The small percentage of testosterone that isn't bound to protein—the fraction that's actually available to your cells.

What it does: This is what actually affects your tissues. All the biological effects come from free testosterone, not total.

Optimal range: 50-210 pg/mL (170-730 pmol/L). Most men feel good in the upper half of this range.

Low means: You can have "normal" total testosterone but low free testosterone if you have high SHBG (see below). This will cause symptoms of low testosterone despite a normal total test. It's a real, treatable problem that many doctors miss.

High means: Less common. Indicates high testosterone production and low SHBG, usually not a problem unless total T is also very high.

Measurement reliability: There are three ways to measure free testosterone: direct (equilibrium dialysis, most accurate, rarely available), calculated (based on total T and SHBG, good enough for clinical purposes), and immunoassay (fast and cheap, but less accurate). Most UK private testing uses calculated. It's fine.

**3. SHBG (Sex Hormone-Binding Globulin)**

What it measures: The protein that binds most of your testosterone and carries it around.

What it does: Regulates how much testosterone is free and available. High SHBG = more testosterone gets bound, less is free. Low SHBG = more free testosterone.

Optimal range: 25-40 nmol/L (roughly). This gives you a good amount of free testosterone.

Low means (below 15 nmol/L): You'll have a lot of free testosterone. If total T is normal, this is usually good. If total T is also high, this can cause problems (very high free T, acne, mood changes, cardiovascular risk). Insulin resistance, obesity, and untreated testosterone therapy can lower SHBG.

High means (above 50 nmol/L): You're binding a lot of testosterone. You might have low free testosterone despite normal or even high total testosterone. This is the hidden culprit for a lot of men. Causes include: genetic predisposition, liver disease, hyperthyroidism, estrogen dominance. You need to fix the underlying cause (if fixable) or potentially consider treatment.

Measurement reliability: Excellent. This is a straightforward protein measurement.

**4. LH (Luteinizing Hormone)**

What it measures: The signal from your pituitary to your testes telling them to make testosterone.

What it does: Stimulates Leydig cells to produce testosterone. If LH is low, your testes aren't getting the signal to make testosterone.

Optimal range: 2-8 IU/L. Most men sit in 4-6.

Low means (below 2 IU/L): Your pituitary isn't stimulating your testes properly. Causes include: high estrogen, hypothyroidism, excessive alcohol, obesity, or problems with the pituitary itself. This needs investigation.

High means (above 10 IU/L): Your pituitary is screaming at your testes to make testosterone, but they're not responding. This indicates a testicular problem (primary hypogonadism) rather than a pituitary problem (secondary hypogonadism).

Measurement reliability: Good, but needs to be timed right. LH pulsates, so a single reading is a snapshot. Ideally, you're fasted and tested in the morning.

**5. FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone)**

What it measures: Another pituitary signal, this one primarily involved in sperm production.

What it does: Stimulates the Sertoli cells in your testes to produce sperm.

Optimal range: 1-8 IU/L. Most men sit in 3-5.

Low means: Usually indicates an issue with the pituitary or hypothalamus. Less clinically relevant than LH and testosterone, but useful for the full picture.

High means: Your pituitary is signalling hard, but sperm production isn't responding. Indicates a primary testicular problem.

Measurement reliability: Good. Similar caveats as LH—pulsatile hormone, morning test is better.

**6. Oestradiol (Estradiol)**

What it measures: The primary female sex hormone. Yes, men have it. It's important.

What it does: In small amounts, it's protective (bone health, sexual function, mood). In large amounts, it causes water retention, gynecomastia (breast tissue growth), mood changes, sexual dysfunction.

Optimal range: 20-60 pg/mL (70-220 pmol/L). Most men feel best in the 30-50 range.

Low means (below 15 pg/mL): Rare in non-treated men. If you're on TRT, low estradiol causes poor mood, poor sexual function, poor joint health, and fragile bones. Common mistake: using AI (aromatase inhibitors) to crash estradiol.

High means (above 80 pg/mL): You have too much estrogen activity. Causes: obesity (fat tissue makes estrogen), heavy alcohol use, liver problems, or if on TRT, not enough aromatase inhibition. Results in soft fat distribution, water retention, low libido, gynecomastia.

Measurement reliability: Reasonable at high levels. Less accurate at low levels (below 20). Most UK testing uses immunoassay, which is fine for clinical purposes.

**7. Prolactin**

What it measures: A hormone made by your pituitary that stimulates milk production in women and suppresses testosterone in men.

What it does: Elevates when you sleep, when you're stressed, when you're in pain. High prolactin directly suppresses testosterone and LH.

Optimal range: 3-12 ng/mL (roughly). Below 10 is what you want.

Low means: Rare and usually not a problem.

High means (above 15 ng/mL): You have elevated prolactin. This causes low testosterone, low libido, erectile dysfunction, mood changes, and fatigue. Causes include: stress, sleep deprivation, dopamine antagonists (some antipsychotics), pituitary problems, or occasionally medication side effects. If elevated, investigate the cause.

Measurement reliability: Good, but needs to be fasted and in the morning (prolactin is highest in morning and after sleep).

**8. Cortisol**

What it measures: Your primary stress hormone.

What it does: In small amounts, it's necessary (wakes you up, motivates you, helps you handle stress). In large amounts, it suppresses testosterone, causes belly fat, wrecks sleep, and contributes to depression.

Optimal range: For morning cortisol: 10-20 µg/dL (270-550 nmol/L). For evening: 2-5 µg/dL (50-140 nmol/L). The drop from morning to evening should be at least 50%.

Low means: Adrenal insufficiency. Rare but serious. Causes fatigue, low blood pressure, low motivation. Needs medical attention.

High means (morning cortisol above 25): You're chronically stressed, sleep-deprived, overtraining, or undereating. Your testosterone is being suppressed. This is extremely common in busy professionals.

Measurement reliability: Very sensitive to testing conditions. You need to be fasted, in the morning, and collected at the right time. Home saliva tests are more convenient; blood tests are more reliable.

**9. DHEA-S (Dehydroepiandrosterone Sulphate)**

What it measures: An adrenal hormone, precursor to testosterone and estrogen.

What it does: Declines with age. Supports testosterone production.

Optimal range: 200-500 µg/dL (5-14 µmol/L) for men 40-50.

Low means: Associated with aging, stress, and poor health. If it's very low, it's a sign of adrenal stress.

High means: Usually not a clinical problem. Can indicate the adrenals are working hard.

Measurement reliability: Good.

**10. Thyroid (TSH, Free T4, Free T3)**

What it measures: Your thyroid function.

What it does: Regulates metabolism, energy, temperature, cognition. Affects testosterone. A sluggish thyroid will slow everything down.

Optimal range: TSH 1-2 mIU/L (the lower end of normal is usually where you feel best). Free T4: 12-20 pmol/L. Free T3: 3.5-6 pmol/L.

Low means (high TSH, low free T): Hypothyroidism. You're tired, brain-foggy, gaining weight, cold. Suppresses testosterone. Treatable with thyroid replacement.

High means: Hyperthyroidism (less common). You're anxious, wired, losing weight, hot. Can also suppress testosterone.

Measurement reliability: TSH is standard. Free T4 and Free T3 are more informative but less commonly tested. If TSH is normal and you still feel terrible, ask for free T4 and T3.

### How to Prepare for Your Test

Most of what matters here is timing and lifestyle. Your hormones fluctuate, so standardizing when you test matters.

**Timing:**

Do your test in the morning. Testosterone peaks early (6-8am for most men), and it's the only time you can catch an accurate snapshot. By afternoon, it's 30% lower. Testing at 3pm and comparing to someone's 8am test is comparing apples to oranges.

Cortisol also peaks in the morning and should be tested then.

If you're using a home collection kit, the instructions will specify. Usually it's first thing after waking, before you eat or drink anything except water.

**Fasting:**

Be fasted (8-12 hours without food). This standardizes some markers and is usually a requirement. Water is fine.

**Day of week:**

Not critical, but weekday mornings are ideal (less weekend variation in cortisol, sleep, etc.).

**Lifestyle:**

The few days before your test, don't dramatically change anything. Don't suddenly sleep 10 hours, don't go hard in the gym, don't fast for days. You want a snapshot of your real life, not your best-case scenario.

Sleep: Get normal sleep the night before (7-9 hours). Poor sleep the night before will elevate cortisol and might affect other markers.

Exercise: Don't do intense exercise the morning of the test. It elevates testosterone temporarily. Light walk is fine.

Supplements: Most supplements are fine to take. If you're on any that might affect results (some adaptogens, any hormonal stuff), you can either skip them for 48 hours or list them when you submit your results so the clinic can note it.

Alcohol: Avoid for 24 hours before the test if possible. Alcohol affects multiple markers (cortisol, free T, liver function). One night of heavy drinking might skew results.

**Supplements to skip before testing:**

If you're already on D3, magnesium, or basic supplements, leave them. If you're on anything marketed as a "testosterone booster," skip it for 48 hours before testing so you know your baseline without it.

### How to Read Your Results

When your results come back, you'll get a PDF with markers, your results, and reference ranges. Here's how to interpret it.

**Reference Range vs Optimal Range**

Remember the distinction. The reference range is what's "statistically normal." The optimal range is where you personally feel good. Your result might be in the reference range and still not optimal for you.

Example: Total testosterone reference range is 10-28 nmol/L. You test at 12 nmol/L (about 350 ng/dL). You're "in range." But most men feel better above 15 nmol/L (450 ng/dL). You might be technically normal and still symptomatic.

**Reading the specific markers:**

Total Testosterone: You want 15-24 nmol/L (450-700 ng/dL). If it's below 10, you have low testosterone. If it's 10-15, it's borderline low. If it's below your personal baseline (if you have one), that's still worth noting.

Free Testosterone: Calculate it if it's not given. Use an online calculator (search "free testosterone calculator"). If calculated free T is below 40 pg/mL despite normal total T, you likely have high SHBG and that's your problem.

SHBG: Above 40 is high. Below 20 is low. If your SHBG is very high and your free T is low despite normal total T, that's the story. High SHBG can be improved with exercise, lower body fat, and better insulin sensitivity.

LH and FSH: Both should be in the normal range (2-8 and 1-8 respectively). If both are low despite low testosterone, your problem is central (pituitary/hypothalamus). If they're elevated (LH high, testosterone low), your problem is testicular. This distinction matters for treatment options.

Prolactin: Should be below 10 ng/mL. If it's above 12, investigate. High prolactin is easily fixable (reduce stress, sleep better, sometimes medication).

Cortisol: Morning cortisol above 20 µg/dL suggests chronic stress. This is suppressing your testosterone. Worth addressing before other interventions.

Oestradiol: 30-50 pg/mL is ideal. Below 20 suggests your testosterone might have been suppressed by other factors (stress, poor diet). Above 60 suggests conversion to estrogen is high (often from excess body fat or alcohol). In context of total testosterone level, you can usually tell whether it's appropriate.

DHEA-S: If it's below 150 µg/dL in a 40+ man, it suggests adrenal stress.

Thyroid (TSH, Free T4): TSH should be below 2.5 (ideally 1-2). If TSH is above 3, you might be hypothyroid. Free T4 should be in the upper half of normal.

**The pattern interpretation:**

Individual results matter less than patterns. Example:

Total T = 12 nmol/L (350 ng/dL) → low
Free T = 25 pg/mL → low
SHBG = 55 nmol/L → HIGH
LH = 5 IU/L → normal
Cortisol (morning) = 22 µg/dL → high
Prolactin = 8 ng/mL → normal

**Reading:** Your total testosterone isn't terrible, but you have high SHBG binding it up, so your free testosterone is low. Your LH is fine, so your pituitary is okay. But your cortisol is elevated, which is both suppressing testosterone production and driving SHBG up. The fix here: address stress, sleep, training intensity. The SHBG might come down and free T might improve without any treatment. Alternatively, you could add a testosterone protocol if symptoms are severe. But trying to boost testosterone without addressing the cortisol is like bailing water out of a boat with a hole in it.

Another example:

Total T = 18 nmol/L (520 ng/dL) → good
Free T = 65 pg/mL → good
SHBG = 30 nmol/L → normal
LH = 2 IU/L → LOW
FSH = 1.5 IU/L → LOW
Cortisol (morning) = 25 µg/dL → HIGH
Thyroid (TSH) = 3.1 mIU/L → slightly high

**Reading:** Your testosterone is fine, but your LH is low. Your pituitary isn't signalling your testes strongly. This could be because of high stress (the elevated cortisol) and the slightly elevated TSH (sluggish thyroid suppresses LH). The fix: address stress and sleep, get thyroid checked properly, consider thyroid replacement if hypothyroid. This might restore LH and normalize everything else. Only consider TRT if these don't improve things after 8 weeks.

Dave comes back with these results:

Total Testosterone: 13.2 nmol/L (382 ng/dL) — Reference 8-28 (so "normal")
Free Testosterone: 28 pg/mL — Reference 30-100 (LOW)
SHBG: 48 nmol/L — Reference 20-60 (high-normal)
LH: 4.2 IU/L — Reference 2-8 (normal)

**Analysis:**

**What Dave should do:**

First, address the modifiable factors:
- Sleep: Aim for 8+ hours consistently. His cortisol is just at the limit.
- Exercise: Resistance training 4x/week at moderate intensity. Avoid chronic cardio, which elevates cortisol. Dave is probably running 5 miles 4x/week to "stay healthy," which is actually suppressing his testosterone.
- Diet: Ensure adequate calories, protein (1.6g/kg), and carbs around training. If Dave is undereating to stay lean, stop.
- Stress: He's likely managing business stress poorly. One day a week completely off. Meditation or breathwork 10min daily.

Second, he could optimize with supplementation:
- Magnesium Glycinate 400mg at night (helps sleep and cortisol).
- Vitamin D3 if not in sun regularly (supports testosterone).
- Creatine 5g daily if training hard.

In 8-12 weeks, retest. If cortisol comes down, DHEA-S rises, and SHBG normalizes, his free testosterone might come back into range without any other intervention.

If he's tried all that and still symptomatic, then consider:
- TRT (might be offered via NHS if he tests below 8 nmol/L after this; private TRT is available)
- Or just see a private consultant for clinical opinion

Dave is a realistic case. He doesn't have severe hypogonadism. He has mild testosterone decline in the context of chronic mild stress and potentially lifestyle factors he can change. A good proportion of his problem is fixable without medication.

### Decision Tree: What to Do With Your Results

**If total testosterone is below 8 nmol/L (<230 ng/dL):**

Go to your GP. You have objective hypogonadism. You may be eligible for NHS TRT. Whether NHS offers it depends on your symptoms and whether you've tried lifestyle changes. Either way, TRT is a reasonable discussion.

**If total testosterone is 8-12 nmol/L (230-350 ng/dL) and you're symptomatic:**

Your testosterone is low. The NHS might not treat you (depends on symptoms, GP, local guidelines). You have options: try the lifestyle optimizations in Chapters 4-7 for 8-12 weeks first. Retest. If no improvement and you're still symptomatic, consider private TRT.

**If total testosterone is 12-15 nmol/L (350-450 ng/dL) and you're symptomatic:**

You're in a grey zone. Your total T is "fine" but possibly lower than your personal baseline. Check your free testosterone and SHBG. If free T is low, address SHBG (exercise, reduce body fat, improve insulin sensitivity). If free T is fine but you're still symptomatic, the problem might not be testosterone. Consider: sleep apnea, depression, thyroid issues, chronic overtraining. Try lifestyle fixes. TRT is unlikely to be covered by NHS but might help; probably worth trying the fundamentals first.

**If total testosterone is above 15 nmol/L (450+ ng/dL) and you're symptomatic:**

You probably don't have low testosterone. Your problem is probably something else: sleep, stress, thyroid, overtraining, depression, poor diet, or relationship/life issues. Don't blame testosterone. Look elsewhere.

**If total testosterone is fine but free testosterone is low (<40 pg/mL):**

You have high SHBG. Address this: exercise (especially resistance training), reduce body fat, improve insulin sensitivity (less refined carbs, more fiber). In 12 weeks, retest. Often improves significantly.

**If prolactin is elevated (>12 ng/mL):**

First, rule out meds (antipsychotics, some antidepressants, domperidone can raise prolactin). Then address: sleep (prioritise it), stress management, dopamine support (L-tyrosine 1-2g daily if appropriate; or simply fixing sleep and stress). Retest in 6 weeks. If still elevated, see a doctor (could be pituitary adenoma, though rare).

**If cortisol is elevated (morning >20 µg/dL):**

This is suppressing your testosterone and everything else. Address: sleep first (most important lever), exercise moderation (you're probably overdoing it), stress management (meditation, time off, saying no to things), calendar auditing (overcommitted?). Retest in 6 weeks. If cortisol comes down, testosterone often improves.

**If thyroid is off (TSH >2.5 or Free T4 in lower half of range):**

See your GP. Hypothyroidism is common and easily treated. It's also suppressing your testosterone and making you feel terrible. Get on replacement (levothyroxine 25-50 mcg to start) and retest in 6-8 weeks.

**If DHEA-S is very low (<150 µg/dL):**

This suggests adrenal stress. Address stress and sleep (same as cortisol). Consider adaptogen support (ashwagandha 500mg 2x daily, or rhodiola 300-600mg morning). Retest in 8 weeks.

# CHAPTER 4: SLEEP IS THE PROTOCOL

Listen, I'm going to start this chapter with something that might sound backwards: most men trying to optimise their hormones are destroying them by not sleeping.

You know the profile. A bloke decides he's going to get his testosterone sorted. He reads something online about needing to train harder, so he adds an extra gym session. He reads about waking up early being more "disciplined," so he cuts sleep to 6.5 hours to fit it all in. He reads about red-light exposure or whatever, so he's in bed checking his phone until 11pm despite knowing he needs to wake at 5am. He's optimising—or so he thinks—while systematically dismantling the one thing that actually makes testosterone possible.

It's the most common and most fixable mistake I see.

Here's the truth, and I'm saying it clearly because it's the most important thing in this entire book: sleep is where testosterone is made. Not in the gym. Not in your food. In sleep. Everything else in this book is secondary.

## The Science of Sleep and Testosterone

When you sleep, your body does something remarkable. Your pituitary gland releases a surge of luteinizing hormone (LH), which tells your testes to produce testosterone. This happens in pulses, and the biggest pulses happen during sleep—specifically during the deep slow-wave sleep phase and into REM sleep. If you're not getting good sleep, you're not getting these pulses. You're not making testosterone.

In 2011, Eve Van Cauter and Karine Leproult published a study that should have made headlines everywhere but didn't, probably because it was slightly terrifying. They took a group of young healthy men (late twenties on average) and restricted them to 5 hours of sleep per night for one week. Not a lifetime of bad sleep—one week. After seven nights of that, their testosterone dropped 10-15%.

Let me repeat that. One week of 5-hour nights: 10-15% testosterone drop in young men. Young. These were men whose systems are much more robust than yours and mine at 40+.

When you extrapolate that conservatively to men in their 40s and 50s—whose testosterone production is already naturally declining—that's a disaster. If a 45-year-old man with baseline testosterone of 450 ng/dL cuts his sleep to 5 hours, he could be looking at 380-400 ng/dL. That's not subclinical. That's a real change.

And here's what makes it worse: most men don't just do it once. They chronically sleep poorly. Year after year of 6-hour nights, of fragmented sleep, of not prioritising sleep because they're "too busy" or "too driven" or "need to get up for a morning workout." The cumulative effect is testosterone that's slowly been crushed.

## Sleep Architecture and Hormone Production

You need to understand what's happening in your sleep, not just how long you're sleeping.

Sleep has stages. When you first fall asleep, you enter light sleep (stages 1 and 2). Your brain is still somewhat responsive, your body temperature is starting to drop, your breathing is slowing. This is fine, but it's not where the magic happens. Light sleep is about 50% of your total sleep time, and it's mostly about basic recovery and memory consolidation.

Then you drop into deep sleep, also called slow-wave sleep or stage 3 sleep. This is where your brain actually switches off in a meaningful way. Your muscles relax completely. Your growth hormone releases in powerful pulses—70% or more of your daily GH production happens during deep sleep. Your body temperature drops further. And critically, your pituitary releases LH, triggering testosterone production. Deep sleep is typically about 15-20% of total sleep in healthy young men, but it declines with age. By your 40s, you might only be getting 10-15% if you're healthy. If you're getting poor sleep, it might be even less. This is why old men often have lower testosterone—they're getting less deep sleep.

Then you cycle into REM sleep (rapid eye movement sleep). This is where dreams happen, where your brain processes information and consolidates memories. REM is crucial for mood, motivation, and psychological recovery. It's also when another pulse of testosterone can happen. REM is about 20-25% of sleep in young men, and it also declines with age and with poor sleep. A man sleeping poorly might be getting 10-15% REM instead of 25%—that's a significant loss of hormonal pulses.

The thing is: you can't shortcut this. You can't get the benefits of deep sleep by sleeping a little deeper for less time. Your brain has a schedule. You need all the stages, in the right proportions, for enough time.

Here's the practical implication: if you're sleeping 6 hours instead of 8, you're not just losing 25% of sleep. You're often losing disproportionately more slow-wave sleep. Your body prioritises REM sleep to some degree (it catches up if you've been deprived), but deep sleep is harder to recover. So you could lose 30-40% of your deep sleep from a 25% reduction in total sleep time. Given that deep sleep is where most of your testosterone pulse happens, this is catastrophic for your hormones.

That matters for testosterone.

## What Destroys Sleep Quality

Your sleep architecture doesn't live in isolation. It gets destroyed by specific modern behaviours. Most men I talk to don't have a problem with sleep quantity—they can sleep 7 or 8 hours—but their sleep quality is atrocious. They're not getting the right stages. Here's what's happening.

**Alcohol.** This is the big one because it's socially acceptable and feels like it helps. Alcohol does help you fall asleep. It's sedating. But once you're asleep, it's catastrophic. Alcohol suppresses REM sleep, and it does so in a dose-dependent manner. A man who has three beers with dinner might sleep for 8 hours but get half his normal REM sleep. He'll wake up feeling like he's had one-eighth of a night's recovery. If you're doing this regularly—even twice a week—you're losing serious sleep quality and thus testosterone recovery.

The evidence here is clear and consistent. Alcohol reduces REM sleep in a dose-dependent way. Two drinks suppresses it somewhat. Five drinks seriously impairs it. And your body prioritises REM recovery over deep sleep recovery, so chronic alcohol use means you're stuck in a cycle of poor deep sleep and decent REM but never fully recovering either. If you're serious about testosterone optimisation, heavy drinking is incompatible with it.

**Blue light.** Your phone, your laptop, your TV. If you're looking at these within an hour of bed, you're suppressing melatonin production. Melatonin isn't magic—it doesn't put you to sleep and it doesn't improve your testosterone directly. But it regulates your circadian rhythm. It tells your body it's time to be tired. If you're suppressing it by staring at blue light, you're staying alert when you should be winding down.

The evidence: blue light exposure in the evening delays sleep onset and shifts your circadian rhythm. That's well-established. Your body's clock is set partially by light exposure, and evening blue light confuses the signal.

**Bedroom temperature.** Your body needs to cool down to sleep well. Your core body temperature drops when you sleep, and that drop is part of the trigger for good sleep architecture. If your bedroom is 22°C (72°F) instead of 18-19°C (64-66°F), you're fighting your physiology. You'll sleep, but it'll be fragmented. Less deep sleep. More waking in the night.

The ideal temperature is 18-19°C. I know that sounds cold. It is. You'll adjust within a week, and your sleep quality will be noticeably better. If you can't achieve this with your central heating, blackout curtains, and opening windows, a cooling mattress topper is a worthwhile investment.

**Stress and high cortisol.** If your cortisol is elevated when you're trying to sleep, sleep is fragmented. Your sympathetic nervous system is activated. You're more likely to wake in the night. You're more likely to have poor REM sleep. We'll deal with cortisol in depth in Chapter 7, but sleep is where it manifests first.

This is a vicious cycle: poor sleep raises your daytime cortisol, which makes sleep worse, which raises cortisol more. Breaking this cycle is crucial for testosterone recovery.

**Shift work.** If you're working nights or rotating shifts, you're fighting your entire circadian system. Your testosterone is tied to your circadian rhythm. If you're not sleeping when your body expects to sleep, your testosterone production is compromised. This is one of the few situations where I'm sympathetic to blaming external circumstances. If you're genuinely shift-working, your testosterone will be lower than it should be, and there's only so much you can do about it. You need to be more diligent with the other factors.

## The Cortisol-Testosterone Morning Rhythm

Here's something most people don't know but should: cortisol and testosterone have an inverse relationship on a circadian basis.

In the morning, cortisol should spike. That's natural and healthy. Cortisol wakes you up, increases your blood glucose, alerts your nervous system. You need that spike. At the same time, testosterone should be declining from its overnight peak. These are inversely correlated on a circadian rhythm.

The problem comes when your sleep is poor. Poor sleep dysregulates cortisol—it doesn't spike properly in the morning, or it stays elevated all day. And when cortisol is dysregulated, testosterone production gets suppressed. Your HPA axis (hypothalamus-pituitary-adrenal, the stress system) is always on high alert, and your HPG axis (hypothalamus-pituitary-gonadal, the testosterone system) gets deprioritised.

This is why poor sleep doesn't just affect you through growth hormone and direct testosterone production. It affects you through stress hormone dysregulation, which then suppresses testosterone throughout the day.

Fix your sleep, and you fix your cortisol profile. Your cortisol rhythms normalise. Your testosterone starts to be produced properly again.

## The Practical Sleep Protocol

I'm going to give you a protocol. Not everything in this list is absolutely essential, but if you do these things, your sleep will improve, and your testosterone will follow.

**Room temperature: 18-19°C.** This is non-negotiable. It should feel slightly cool when you get into bed. You'll warm up. Within a week you'll sleep noticeably better. If you can't cool your entire room that much, a good mattress topper with cooling gel, or even just opening windows at night, helps. Some men find a fan pointed away from the bed helps with circulating cool air.

**Complete darkness.** Not "mostly dark." Completely dark. If you can see your hand in front of your face, it's too light. Blackout blinds, or an eye mask—whatever works. Light exposure at night suppresses melatonin. Melatonin regulates your circadian rhythm. Don't compromise on this. Even a small bedside light or the glow from a digital alarm clock can suppress melatonin and degrade sleep quality.

**No screens 60 minutes before bed.** This is standard advice because it works. Blue light delays sleep onset and impairs REM sleep. One hour before bed: put the phone away. Read, talk to your partner, do something relaxing. Your sleep onset will improve, and your sleep quality will be noticeably better. If you can't do a full hour, 30 minutes is better than nothing.

**Consistent wake time.** This matters more than consistent bedtime. Your circadian rhythm is stronger to wake time than sleep time. If you wake at 7am every day—even weekends—your cortisol will start to spike before 7am. Your body will anticipate waking. You'll sleep better because your body knows what to expect. Sleeping in on weekends sounds nice, but it destabilises your rhythm and worsens your sleep quality the rest of the week. A consistent wake time is more powerful than most men realise.

**Magnesium glycinate, 300-400mg before bed.** This is the one supplement I'd give top priority to for sleep. Magnesium glycinate is the form that crosses the blood-brain barrier efficiently and is well-absorbed. The evidence: magnesium supplementation improves sleep quality and reduces sleep onset latency. People sleep deeper and feel more rested. 300-400mg taken 1-2 hours before bed is standard. It's cheap (£10-15/month for a good product). Don't use magnesium oxide—it's poorly absorbed and causes digestive upset. Glycinate is the form that matters.

**Consistent sleep schedule.** Aim for 7-8.5 hours, with 7.5-8.5 being optimal for most men. You can test your individual optimum by seeing what sleep length gives you the best energy and recovery for a week. Most men over 40 need at least 7.5 hours to optimise testosterone production. Some need 8.5 hours. This isn't laziness—it's biology.

## What NOT to Do

**Sleeping pills.** I know this might sound controversial, but sleeping pills are counterproductive for long-term hormone health. Most prescription sleeping pills (benzodiazepines, Z-drugs like zopiclone) suppress REM sleep and deep sleep. They make you unconscious, but they don't give you good sleep architecture. A study in older adults showed that people on benzodiazepines for sleep actually had worse sleep quality, more fragmented sleep, and poorer daytime function than untreated insomnia. If you're desperate enough for sleeping pills that you're considering them, you need to fix the underlying causes: stress, caffeine timing, temperature, light, alcohol, or a sleep disorder like sleep apnea. Sleeping pills should be absolute last resort, not first resort.

**Melatonin as a nightly sleep aid.** Melatonin is a circadian rhythm hormone. It tells your body what time it is. It's genuinely useful for jet lag (shifting your circadian rhythm to a new time zone). It's not useful for chronic insomnia. Taking melatonin every night doesn't improve sleep quality; it just shifts your rhythm and your body downregulates its own melatonin production. If your rhythm is already correct (you're in your normal time zone, you go to bed at a normal time), melatonin adds nothing. Save it for travel.

## Personal Tracking and Data

I use an Oura Ring to track my sleep. I know not everyone wants to buy a wearable, and you don't strictly need one, but if you're trying to fix sleep, data is incredibly useful.

When I first started really optimising sleep three years ago, my average deep sleep was about 45 minutes per night. My REM sleep was around 55 minutes. My HRV (heart rate variability, a marker of stress and recovery) was in the mid-40s. I was getting 7-7.5 hours most nights, but the quality wasn't there.

When I fixed the basics—temperature to 18°C, magnesium glycinate, no screens after 9pm, consistent wake time at 6:30am—within two weeks my deep sleep went to 65 minutes. My REM sleep stayed around 50-60 minutes. My HRV climbed to mid-50s. I felt noticeably better. Energy improved. Recovery improved. Libido improved.

Now, three years later, I average 75 minutes of deep sleep, 70 minutes of REM sleep, and HRV in the mid-60s on a good night (it varies based on stress). That's not because I'm genetically gifted at sleeping. It's because I systematically removed the things that were destroying sleep quality.

This data—seeing it improve week by week—was what actually kept me consistent. It's hard to care about sleep quality as an abstract concept. Seeing it quantified, seeing your deep sleep climb, your HRV improve, and knowing that these metrics correlate with better testosterone production: that's motivating.

If you don't have a wearable, you can roughly track this by how you feel. Do you wake groggy or alert? Do you need coffee to function, or do you naturally wake with energy? How's your recovery in the gym? How's your libido? How's your mood? These are proxies for sleep quality. Track the subjective markers.

## The Minimum Effective Dose

Here's what you actually need: a floor of 7 hours, optimal of 7.5-8.5 hours.

That's it. You don't need 10 hours. That might even be counterproductive (oversleeping is sometimes associated with depression and poor metabolic health, though the causality is unclear). You need enough to allow for your full sleep architecture, with enough deep and REM sleep for testosterone production and recovery.

For most men, 7 hours is a bare minimum. You can probably function on it for a few nights. But if that's your chronic level, your testosterone will suffer. 7.5-8 is the sweet spot for most active men. Some people thrive on 7.5; others need 8.5. Spend a week at each and see what gives you the best energy and recovery.

The other thing: sleep debt is real and it compounds. One night of 5-hour sleep doesn't destroy you. Two nights of 6-hour sleep is manageable. But chronic sleep restriction—night after night of 6.5 hours when you need 8—slowly degrades everything. Your cortisol rhythm. Your testosterone production. Your immune function. Your mood. Your motivation. You feel like it's just normal, like this is how you are, and then one day you fix it and realise you've been operating at 70% for years.

# CHAPTER 5: THE TESTOSTERONE DIET

You can't out-supplement a bad diet. You also can't out-diet a bad sleep or bad training programme. But you can wreck your testosterone with a genuinely poor diet, so let's be clear about what actually works.

Here's the honest truth about diet and testosterone: there's no special "testosterone diet." There's no magical food that tanks your test or magically boosts it. What there is—and what people usually miss—is that certain dietary patterns and nutrient profiles support testosterone production, while others actively suppress it. And the differences matter more than you'd think.

The worst part is that many men have internalised the message that fat is bad, so they eat low-fat diets while trying to optimise their hormones. This is backwards. This is the exact opposite of what your body needs.

## Fat and the Testosterone Precursor Chain

Here's a foundational fact that shapes everything else in this chapter: cholesterol is the precursor to all steroid hormones, including testosterone.

Your body takes cholesterol and converts it into pregnenolone, which then converts into testosterone (among other steroids). If you don't have enough cholesterol available, your body can't make testosterone efficiently. This isn't debatable—it's basic biochemistry. It's how your hormonal system is built.

And yet, for decades, we've told men to eat low-fat diets. We've told them fat is bad, cholesterol is bad, and they should minimise both. This is a disaster for testosterone production.

In 1984, Hamalainen and colleagues published a study comparing high-fat and low-fat diets in healthy men. When they shifted men from a high-fat diet (40% of calories) to a low-fat diet (20% of calories), testosterone dropped meaningfully. Free testosterone dropped even more—the biologically active form. The men weren't overweight, they didn't have metabolic disease, they were just eating less fat—and their testosterone production declined.

You'll see this pattern repeatedly in the literature. When fat intake is too low—below about 20-25% of calories—testosterone production is suppressed. It's not linear; you don't need 50% of calories from fat (that's too much for most people and tends to come with too many calories overall). But you do need enough.

The practical implication: if you're eating a diet where fat is deliberately restricted—like a low-fat bodybuilding diet or a "heart-healthy" diet that's aggressively low in fat—you're suppressing your testosterone. If you're trying to optimise hormones while eating 15% of calories from fat, you're working against yourself. You need at least 25-30% of calories from fat, ideally from good sources.

## Specific Foods and Their Evidence

Rather than prescribe a diet structure, let me talk about specific foods with real evidence.

**Red meat.** This is where a lot of the confusion lives because red meat is loaded with things your testosterone pathway needs: zinc, cholesterol, B12, creatine, iron, and saturated fat. The evidence is clear that adequate red meat consumption (roughly 2-3 times per week) is associated with better testosterone levels. Is this because of the meat specifically, or because people who eat red meat are eating enough fat and protein generally? Probably both. But the point is: if you're avoiding red meat out of worry about heart health, you might be harming your testosterone. Lean beef is fine (it still has fat and nutrients), but don't avoid it entirely. Grass-fed beef has slightly different fatty acid ratios, but regular beef is fine too. The point is to eat it.

**Eggs.** Complete protein, cholesterol, choline, lutein, zeaxanthin, all the amino acids your body needs to synthesise testosterone and build muscle. An egg a day minimum. Three or four if you're training hard. There's no research showing that dietary cholesterol from eggs harms your heart (the egg-demonising era was based on flawed research). Eggs are one of the most nutrient-dense foods you can eat, and they're cheap. They also include compounds specifically useful for reproductive and hormonal health.

**Olive oil.** Extra virgin olive oil has polyphenols and monounsaturated fat. There's a Portuguese study showing olive oil consumption was associated with better testosterone levels in men. The mechanism probably involves polyphenols and overall diet quality (olive oil is part of Mediterranean diet patterns). Use it liberally—2-3 tablespoons daily on salads or cooking. It's evidence-supported and adds calories from fat without being excessive. The polyphenols are also anti-inflammatory.

**Cruciferous vegetables.** Broccoli, cabbage, Brussels sprouts, cauliflower—these contain indole-3-carbinol (I3C) and diindolylmethane (DIM), which influence oestrogen metabolism. The theory is that I3C and DIM shift oestrogen metabolism toward less potent forms, which theoretically helps keep your free testosterone higher relative to total testosterone. The evidence base is modest—we don't have large human studies showing that eating broccoli raises testosterone by a meaningful amount. But the biochemistry makes sense, the compounds do have actual effects, and there are no downsides to eating more vegetables. So eat them. A serving or two daily is reasonable.

**Fish and omega-3s.** Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA) are anti-inflammatory, support cardiovascular health, improve blood flow, and are associated with better testosterone levels. You probably need to either eat fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines, anchovies) 2-3 times weekly, or supplement with fish oil. This isn't specifically for testosterone—it's for overall health—but it falls into the "supporting" category. A deficiency in omega-3s is associated with worse inflammatory markers and worse hormone profiles.

## What Suppresses Testosterone

**Excessive alcohol.** We talked about alcohol and sleep. There's also a direct effect. Alcohol suppresses testosterone production acutely (even one night of heavy drinking lowers testosterone for several days) and chronically (regular heavy drinking reduces baseline testosterone). The evidence is very clear here. If you're drinking more than 2-3 units per day on average, or regularly having heavy drinking sessions (5+ drinks in a night), your testosterone is being suppressed. This is one of the clearest dietary interventions: if you drink heavily, stopping will directly improve your testosterone. You don't need to be a teetotaller, but regular heavy drinking is incompatible with testosterone optimisation.

**Heavily processed foods.** The evidence is less specific here, but processed foods tend to be calorie-dense, hyperpalatable, and inflammatory. They contribute to weight gain, chronic inflammation, and poor metabolic health—all of which suppress testosterone. This isn't novel advice, but it's worth restating: whole foods, minimally processed, will support testosterone better than a processed diet. Ultra-processed foods also often contain seed oils high in omega-6, which promote inflammation when not balanced with omega-3s.

**Soy and isoflavones.** This is nuanced and worth getting right because a lot of nonsense circulates about soy. Soy contains isoflavones, which have weak oestrogenic activity. In animal models at high doses, isoflavones can suppress testosterone. But in humans? The evidence doesn't show that normal soy consumption suppresses testosterone. A review of studies on soy and testosterone in humans found that normal consumption (like eating tofu or drinking soy milk occasionally) had no meaningful effect on testosterone. Large amounts (several servings daily over months) showed minimal effects. And importantly: cooking and fermentation reduce isoflavone content. So a bloke eating normal amounts of soy is fine. You don't need to avoid it out of fear. But if you're eating soy at every meal (soy milk, soy protein isolate, tofu daily), you could theoretically suppress testosterone slightly. Probably not by much, but if you're optimising, choose variety.

## Caloric Restriction and Body Composition

Here's something crucial that trips up a lot of men: severe caloric restriction suppresses testosterone.

If you're in a 25%+ caloric deficit, you're almost certainly suppressing testosterone. Your body is in crisis mode. It's prioritising immediate survival over reproduction. Testosterone production falls, cortisol rises. You'll lose weight (including muscle), but you're also harming your hormones. This is counterproductive if your goal is to feel good and maintain testosterone.

A moderate deficit—10-15%—is generally fine. You can lose fat without wrecking your testosterone. But aggressive cutting (more than 20-25% deficit) will hurt you. If you're trying to optimise testosterone, lose fat slowly. Patience wins here. Losing one kilogram per month while maintaining hormonal health beats losing five kilograms per month while tanking your testosterone.

## Protein Intake

Protein matters, though not specifically for testosterone. It matters for muscle synthesis and body composition. The evidence-based range for active men is 1.6-2.2 grams per kilogram of bodyweight daily. For a 90kg man, that's roughly 145-200g of protein daily, depending on your training volume.

More protein than that doesn't seem to provide additional benefit for muscle synthesis, though it might help with satiety if you're dieting. Less than 1.6g/kg probably isn't enough if you're training hard. Get enough protein. It's boring but it matters.

## The Mediterranean Diet as a Template

If I had to point to one dietary pattern with evidence for overall health, cardiovascular function, and reasonable hormonal support, it's the Mediterranean diet: lots of vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, fish, olive oil, moderate poultry, red meat included (though less frequently than fish or poultry), minimal processed foods, moderate wine (if you drink).

It's not a testosterone-specific diet. It's a diet associated with good health outcomes, reasonable weight management, and hormone-friendly macro balance. It naturally tends to be 30-35% fat (from olive oil, fish, and whole foods), moderate protein (from fish, legumes, and poultry), and good carbs (from vegetables, whole grains, fruit).

For a working man trying to optimise testosterone, this is a reasonable template. Not rigid, not obsessive, but sound. It's been studied for decades and the evidence is solid.

## Practical Meal Structure for a Busy Man

You don't need complicated meal plans. You need consistent, non-negotiable meals.

**Breakfast.** Eggs (2-3) with whole-grain toast and olive oil, or oats with protein powder and berries. Something with protein, carbs, and fat. Done. This takes 10 minutes and sets you up for the day.

**Lunch.** A piece of protein (chicken, fish, or beef), a pile of vegetables, and a source of carbs (rice, potato, or whole grain). The "non-negotiable meal." You eat this most days. Boring but works. This is the meal that keeps you consistent, keeps your blood sugar stable, and keeps your energy up.

**Dinner.** Similar structure: protein, vegetables, carbs. Maybe a bit more relaxed than lunch because it's evening, but similar principle. More variety here is fine—cooking it yourself or eating out, different proteins, whatever.

**Snacks.** If you're hungry: handful of nuts, Greek yoghurt, fruit, a piece of cheese. Nothing elaborate. These are energy bridges between meals.

The point: you're not micro-managing macros to the gram. You're building a structure where most days you're eating whole foods, adequate protein, reasonable fat, decent carbs, and tonnes of vegetables. When you eat like this consistently, your testosterone will be supported, your body composition will be reasonable, and you won't spend mental energy obsessing over food.

The men who struggle are the ones trying to do something fancy: intermittent fasting, carnivore, very-low-carb, complicated cycling. These might work for specific goals, but they're not necessary for testosterone optimisation. Consistency beats perfection. A mediocre diet done consistently beats a perfect diet done inconsistently.

## Micronutrients That Matter

While we're talking about diet, a few specific micronutrients deserve mention because they're directly involved in testosterone production and they're often deficient in modern diets.

**Zinc.** Zinc is a cofactor in the enzymes that produce testosterone. Deficiency correlates with low testosterone. You need about 11mg daily (most men get this from food), but if you're training hard, sweating a lot, or living in high stress, you might need more. Red meat, oysters, pumpkin seeds, and nuts are good sources. Don't megadose zinc—more than 25mg daily can actually suppress immunity. Just get enough.

**Vitamin D3.** UK men are deficient. Most of us aren't getting enough sun exposure to make adequate D3, and food sources are limited. Deficiency correlates with low testosterone. Supplementing with 2000-4000 IU daily is reasonable and evidence-supported. Get your level tested if you can (ideally 30-50 ng/mL).

**Selenium.** Selenium is required for thyroid function and antioxidant defense. Deficiency impairs testosterone metabolism. A couple of Brazil nuts daily or adequate whole grains usually covers this, but it's worth knowing about.

The point: you don't need a complicated micronutrient protocol. You need whole foods that include sources of these minerals. Varied diet, whole foods, lots of different vegetables and protein sources, and you're probably covered.

## Dining Out and Social Eating

A real challenge for busy men is eating well when you're not cooking. Work lunches, dinners with clients, social occasions—these are where diet falls apart for most men.

The practical approach: have principles, not rules. Your principle is "whole foods, adequate protein, reasonable portions." When you're dining out, you can follow that principle. Grilled fish or steak with vegetables and rice. You don't need to be perfect. You don't need to send back food or be difficult. You just order food that broadly fits your goals.

The men who struggle are the ones trying to be perfect. They can't have any processed food. They can't deviate. So one pizza at a work event feels like failure, and they give up entirely. Better to have principles that are flexible. You're optimising testosterone, not competing in a bodybuilding show.

## Timing and Meal Frequency

One question I get: does meal timing matter? Does meal frequency matter? Does eating carbs at night matter?

Honestly? It's way down the priority list compared to total intake. Total calories matter. Total macros (adequate protein and fat) matter. Whole foods matter. Whether you eat three meals or six, whether you eat carbs at night or in the morning, matters far less.

That said, most men do better with consistent meal timing and 3-4 meals daily. It keeps blood sugar stable. It keeps energy stable. It's easier to maintain consistency. So build a structure you can stick to and move on.

## The Critical Point

You cannot optimise testosterone with a diet that's too low in fat, too low in calories, or too low in whole foods. But you also don't need a "special" diet. You need consistent, sensible eating of whole foods, adequate protein, enough fat (25-30% of calories minimum), and tonnes of vegetables. That's it.

The men who see the biggest changes in testosterone from diet are usually the ones who were doing something obviously broken: extreme restriction, excessive alcohol, or processed food dependency. Fix the obvious problems and the diet works. You don't need to be perfect.

# CHAPTER 6: TRAIN LIKE YOU MEAN IT

Here's where people get confused: resistance training doesn't raise your testosterone acutely in the way that would change your life. You finish a hard session, your testosterone spikes for 15-30 minutes, and then it's gone. That acute spike isn't what matters.

What matters is the chronic adaptation. When you train hard, with the right stimulus, consistently, your body adapts by increasing its capacity to produce testosterone and improving your androgen receptor sensitivity. It's a long-term upregulation of the system, not a short-term hack. The signal from training to your system is: "we need to be stronger, we need more testosterone to support that adaptation."

But here's the catch: you have to actually train in a way that triggers this adaptation. Most men either train too much (destroying recovery and raising cortisol, which suppresses testosterone), or train too little (not providing enough stimulus). The sweet spot is a specific kind of training, and it's not what most people think.

## What Drives Testosterone Adaptation

The research is pretty clear on what type of training elicits the best hormonal adaptation for testosterone specifically.

**Compound movements.** Squat, deadlift, bench press, rows, overhead press. These exercises use large muscle groups, require heavy weights, and engage your whole nervous system. When you do them, multiple hormonal systems activate. These are not optional. If your training doesn't include compound movements, you're not optimally stimulating testosterone production. Isolation exercises like bicep curls and leg presses are fine as accessories, but the foundation must be compounds.

**Moderate to heavy weights.** We're talking 70-85% of your one-rep max. Not light weight for high reps (better for muscle endurance, not hormone response). Not super-heavy singles that your nervous system can barely handle (more injury risk, harder to recover from). The sweet spot where you can do 5-12 reps per set with good form and progressive tension. This is where the hormonal response is optimized.

**Adequate volume.** A meta-analysis by Brad Schoenfeld (the authority on resistance training for hypertrophy and hormonal response) found that about 10-20 working sets per muscle group per week is optimal for muscle growth and hormonal response. That's working sets—sets where you're actually putting in effort. Not warm-up sets. Not the 15 light sets a bloke doing 20-rep squats might do. Real, challenging sets.

When you combine these three things—compound movements, moderate-heavy weight, adequate volume—your testosterone production increases. Not dramatically, but meaningfully. It's one of the few lifestyle interventions with consistent evidence for supporting testosterone. The literature is consistent: structured resistance training with compounds and progressive overload supports testosterone production.

## The Overtraining Trap

And here's the massive problem most men don't understand: if you exceed a certain training volume, you flip into overtraining territory, and your testosterone gets crushed instead.

The mechanism: overtraining raises cortisol. Cortisol is catabolic and suppresses testosterone. If you're training 6-7 days a week for two hours a day, eating in a deficit, sleeping poorly, and under work stress, your cortisol is sky-high. Your testosterone is suppressed. You're actually harming yourself. You've crossed the threshold where training helps into the territory where it hurts.

I see this constantly. A bloke decides he's going to "get serious" about training. He goes from 3 days a week to 6 days a week. Maybe adds cardio. Cuts calories because he wants to "get lean." Isn't sleeping enough because he's busy. Within four weeks, his testosterone is lower than it was before. He feels worse. He thinks he needs to train harder, so he adds more. It gets worse. It's a downward spiral.

This is the opposite of what he needed. He needed more recovery, better sleep, adequate calories, and maybe slightly more structured training, but not more volume.

The signal that you're overdoing it: you feel flat. Low motivation. Sluggish. Libido drops. Mood drops. Resting heart rate goes up. Sleep quality deteriorates. These aren't signs to train harder. These are signs you're overtraining and your testosterone is being suppressed by elevated cortisol. The fix is to back off. Train less. Sleep more. Eat more. Manage stress.

## Cardiovascular Exercise and Testosterone

Cardiovascular exercise is important. It's good for your heart, for your metabolic health, for your overall fitness. You need some. But chronic excessive endurance exercise can suppress testosterone.

Marathon training, for example, genuinely suppresses testosterone in some men. Very high-volume endurance training raises cortisol and creates a catabolic environment. Your body is in breakdown mode. You're burning through energy, your cortisol is elevated to sustain the effort, and your testosterone gets deprioritised.

But moderate cardiovascular exercise—2-3 sessions per week of moderate intensity, or regular HIIT—is fine. It actually supports overall health, improves blood flow (good for sexual function), and supports recovery without suppressing hormones. The key is moderation. You're not trying to become a distance runner. You're trying to be a healthy guy with good cardiovascular fitness who also has good testosterone.

The practical balance: include 2-3 sessions of moderate cardio per week (20-30 minutes at a conversational pace, or 10-15 minutes of HIIT), but keep the emphasis on resistance training. Resistance training is what drives testosterone adaptation. Cardio is supporting your overall health.

## The Sustainable Programme for Men Over 40

Here's what sustainable training looks like for a man trying to optimise testosterone:

**Three to four sessions per week.** Not five, not six. Four maximum, three minimum. This is enough to stimulate adaptation, enough volume to drive progress, sustainable with work and life commitments. More than four days becomes a lifestyle—you're in the gym more than you're doing other things that matter.

**Each session is 45 minutes to an hour.** Not 90 minutes. You warm up (5-10 minutes), you do your main work (35-45 minutes of actual lifting), you're out. Anything beyond that is mostly ego or poor programming. Your nervous system can only handle so much intensity before quality declines.

**Lower body once per week.** Squat or deadlift as your main movement, supplemented with accessory work (leg press, leg curls, hamstring work). This hits large muscle groups, requires heavy weight, and triggers significant hormonal response.

**Upper body pushing once per week.** Bench press, incline press, or overhead press as the main movement. Something pressing that recruits your chest, shoulders, and triceps.

**Upper body pulling once per week.** Rows, pull-ups, or weighted chin-ups. Something pulling that recruits your back and biceps.

**Optional second session.** If you're doing four days, the fourth is typically a second lower body session with different emphasis (maybe deadlift focus if the first session was squat focus), or another upper body day with different angles.

**Example: four-day split**
- Day 1: Lower body A (squat focus) + accessories
- Day 2: Upper body B (bench focus) + accessories
- Day 3: Rest
- Day 4: Lower body B (deadlift focus) + accessories
- Day 5: Upper body A (row/pull focus) + accessories
- Days 6-7: Rest

Each session includes a compound movement and 15-20 total working sets of resistance work. You rest at least a day between sessions hitting the same muscle group (ideally 48-72 hours).

**Progressive overload:** This is non-negotiable. Every week, you're trying to do slightly more: one more rep on the same weight, or slightly more weight for the same reps. This is what drives adaptation. Without progressive overload, your body doesn't need to adapt. It's the signal that tells your system: "we need to build, we need more testosterone to support rebuilding and recovery." It's the stimulus that matters.

## Recovery Is Longer at 40+

Here's something that changes around 40: recovery takes longer.

A bloke at 25 can do high-frequency training—hitting the same muscle group every three days—and recover fine. His testosterone is high. His nervous system recovers quickly. His protein synthesis is efficient. By 40, this doesn't work as well for most men.

You need more sleep between hard sessions. You need more separation between sessions hitting the same muscle group (72 hours is better than 48 hours for a man over 40). Your warmups take longer. You need more attention to movement quality because you're more prone to injury. A hamstring tear at 25 is a nuisance. A hamstring tear at 45 is a problem that affects your entire program.

This isn't decline. It's just adaptation. Respect it. Programme accordingly. More recovery between sessions, adequate sleep, and thoughtful programming beats maximalist volume every time.

## Warm-Up Matters More

Along the same lines: warm-up routine matters more as you age.

A proper warm-up—5-10 minutes of movement prep, some light activity, some mobility work (especially hip and shoulder mobility if you have desk work), then building up to working weight—reduces injury risk and improves performance. At 25, you could wing it. At 40+, you can't. Spend the time. A 10-minute warmup that prevents a shoulder injury that would sideline you for months is time well spent.

## Personal Experience

Three years ago, my training was 5-6 days a week. I was in the gym more than I was with my family. I was eating in a slight deficit because I was under the impression that "more training = more results." I was sleeping maybe 6.5 hours most nights because I was getting up early to train.

My testosterone was middling. Not terrible, but not good. Energy was fine—I was young-ish and stubborn—but I wasn't feeling great. Libido was OK but not great. Recovery wasn't fast.

When I restructured to 4 days a week, roughly 50 minutes per session, compound-focused, with better recovery between sessions, I expected my results to tank. They didn't. My strength went up. My body composition improved. My recovery improved dramatically. My libido improved. My energy throughout the day improved.

And my testosterone went up 80-90 ng/dL.

The only explanation: I was overtraining before. My cortisol was elevated, suppressing my testosterone. By backing off, giving my body actual recovery, reducing the stress signal from chronic overtraining, I allowed my testosterone system to upregulate.

This is the most common shift I see in men over 40. They're often doing too much, not too little. Their cortisol is high from overtraining on top of life stress. Their sleep is poor because training is exhausting but they're not recovering properly. Their calories are too low because they're trying to cut while also recovering from excessive training. Everything compounds. The fix is to back off training volume, prioritise sleep and calories, and let the system recover.

## Practical Programme Example

Let me give you a concrete example of what 4 days a week actually looks like in practice.

**Monday: Lower Body A (Squat focus)**
- Warmup: 5 minutes rowing, mobility work
- Back Squat: 4 sets x 5-8 reps (heavy)
- Leg Press: 3 sets x 8-10 reps
- Leg Curls: 3 sets x 10-12 reps
- Core: 2 sets of planks or ab wheel
- Total: ~50 minutes

**Wednesday: Upper Body Push (Bench focus)**
- Warmup: mobility, light rowing
- Bench Press: 4 sets x 5-8 reps (heavy)
- Incline Dumbbell Press: 3 sets x 8-10 reps
- Lateral Raises: 3 sets x 12 reps
- Tricep Dips: 2 sets x 8-10 reps
- Total: ~45 minutes

**Friday: Lower Body B (Deadlift focus)**
- Warmup: 5 minutes rowing, mobility
- Deadlift: 4 sets x 3-5 reps (heavy)
- Leg Press: 3 sets x 8-10 reps
- Leg Extensions: 3 sets x 10-12 reps
- Hamstring Curls: 2 sets x 10 reps
- Total: ~50 minutes

**Sunday: Upper Body Pull (Rowing focus)**
- Warmup: light cardio, mobility
- Barbell Rows: 4 sets x 5-8 reps (heavy)
- Weighted Chin-ups: 3 sets x 5-8 reps
- Cable Rows: 3 sets x 10 reps
- Barbell Curls: 2 sets x 8 reps
- Face Pulls: 2 sets x 15 reps
- Total: ~50 minutes

That's your template. You're hitting each muscle group once weekly with a heavy compound, supporting it with volume, and managing recovery by spacing sessions appropriately. Progressive overload: add a rep or weight each week where you can.

## Deload Weeks

One thing I didn't mention: every 4-6 weeks, take a deload week. Reduce volume by about 40-50%. Train lighter. Let your nervous system recover. Your joints recover. Your hormones normalise. Contrary to what some gym culture says, deload weeks are when adaptation happens. You signal to your body: okay, we're safe now, let's build. Then you come back stronger.

## Injury Prevention

At 40+, avoiding injury is part of the game. A serious injury can sideline your training for months. Here's the practical approach:

- Warmup every session, even light sessions
- Use quality of movement, not ego. The weight doesn't matter; the stimulus matters
- Don't PR (personal record) every week. Build gradually
- Take care of nagging injuries before they become serious. A sore shoulder that you ignore becomes a shoulder that can't bench for 3 months
- Mobility work: 5-10 minutes before training focusing on areas you need
- Listen to your body. Soreness is fine. Sharp pain is not

## The Bottom Line

Resistance training with compound movements, moderate-heavy weight, adequate volume (but not excessive), and proper recovery, will support testosterone production as well as anything in this book.

But "training hard" is not the same as "training smart." Training hard and thoughtlessly will crush your testosterone. Training smart—moderate frequency, good technique, progressive overload, proper recovery—will support it.

And remember: training is one variable. It's not more important than sleep, diet, or stress management. If you're training perfectly but sleeping 6 hours, your testosterone will still be suppressed. If you're training perfectly but eating in a severe deficit, your testosterone will be suppressed. If you're training perfectly but under chronic stress, your testosterone will be suppressed. The foundations matter first. Training is important but it's not the foundation.

# CHAPTER 7: THE CORTISOL TRAP

You can do everything right—sleep 8 hours, train smart, eat well, take your supplements—and still have suppressed testosterone if your cortisol is chronically elevated.

This is the invisible ceiling. Most men don't even know they're hitting it.

Cortisol is your stress hormone. It's not evil, it's necessary, but modern life keeps it elevated constantly, and when cortisol is chronically high, your testosterone system gets deprioritised. This is a trade-off your body makes at a biological level: under threat, focus on survival, not reproduction. Your testosterone production is luxury spending in an economy of scarcity.

## The HPA Axis and the Testosterone Trade-Off

Your stress response is mediated by the HPA axis: hypothalamus (in your brain) sends a signal to your pituitary gland, which releases ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone), which signals your adrenal glands to release cortisol.

This system evolved to handle acute threats. A predator, a fight, something that required an immediate survival response. Cortisol spikes, your blood glucose rises (energy for running), your nervous system activates, your digestion shuts down (you don't need to digest when running from a threat), you either fight or run. Then the threat passes, cortisol drops, your body goes back to normal, and your testosterone can be made again.

The problem is that your body can't distinguish between a predator and your mortgage, your boss, your commute, your upcoming presentation, or your email inbox. It treats them all the same. If you're experiencing chronic low-grade stress—and most professional men are—your cortisol is constantly slightly elevated.

When this happens, your body makes a choice: focus on survival or reproduction? Your testosterone system—which is part of the HPG axis (hypothalamus-pituitary-gonadal, the testosterone pathway)—is deprioritised. Your body reasons: we might not survive, so let's not invest energy in making testosterone for reproduction. We'll deal with that later. Survival first, sex later.

This is the HPA/HPG trade-off, and it's why stressed men have low testosterone. It's not a defect. It's biological programming. Your body is doing exactly what it's supposed to do under threat. The problem is that modern stress is chronic, not acute.

## The Cortisol-Testosterone Relationship

There's a daily rhythm to cortisol and testosterone that's often disrupted in modern life.

In the morning, cortisol should spike. This is healthy. It wakes you up, increases your blood glucose, activates your nervous system, mobilises energy. It's part of your normal circadian rhythm. At the same time, testosterone should be declining from its overnight peak. These two hormones are inversely correlated on a circadian basis. They're supposed to move in opposite directions.

Throughout the day, cortisol should decline gradually. Your body calms down. Testosterone gradually rises slightly. In the evening, cortisol should be low, allowing you to wind down. Overnight, testosterone rises to its peak as cortisol is minimal.

When you have chronic stress, this rhythm breaks down. Cortisol doesn't decline properly during the day. It stays elevated. Testosterone production is suppressed throughout the day. You might wake feeling unwell, never fully recover during the day (because your nervous system is still activated), and then can't sleep properly at night because your cortisol is still elevated in the evening (your nervous system can't calm down).

This is a vicious cycle. Poor sleep makes cortisol worse (cortisol dysregulation impairs sleep architecture), which makes sleep worse, which makes cortisol worse. You get stuck in a loop of elevated cortisol and poor sleep and suppressed testosterone.

## Modern Cortisol Problem

The stress response evolved for acute, physical threats. But modern stress is chronic, psychological, and often inescapable.

You can't "run from" a difficult project at work. You can't "fight" your commute. You can't physically resolve financial worry. You can't exercise away a difficult relationship or career uncertainty. So the stress response stays activated, cortisol stays elevated, and your testosterone system stays suppressed.

And here's what makes it worse: many men are also doing things that compound stress. They're sleep-deprived (raises cortisol and impairs recovery). They're overtraining (raises cortisol, adds physical stress on top of mental stress). They're in caloric deficit (raises cortisol, signals bodily threat). They're constantly stimulated by work email and social media (raises cortisol, keeps nervous system activated). They're not taking real breaks (cortisol never has a chance to drop). They're isolated (social connection lowers cortisol). Their cortisol is elevated from multiple sources, and their testosterone is getting crushed from multiple angles.

## Signs of Chronically Elevated Cortisol

If you have several of these, your cortisol is likely elevated:

- Difficulty losing abdominal fat despite diet and training. Cortisol preferentially deposits fat viscerally (around your organs, contributing to the "apple shape") and makes stubborn belly fat harder to shift. Chronically elevated cortisol plus elevated insulin (from stress eating or constant grazing) is a recipe for stubborn visceral fat.
- Poor sleep quality despite being tired. You lie awake racing thoughts, you wake in the night, you wake groggy feeling unrefreshed. This is cortisol-suppressed sleep architecture. Your nervous system is too activated.
- Low motivation and drive. Not clinical depression, but a lack of purpose or enthusiasm. Cortisol-driven. You have no energy for things you used to enjoy.
- Low libido. Elevated cortisol suppresses testosterone and sexual motivation. It's hard to be interested in sex when your body thinks you're being hunted.
- Elevated fasting blood glucose. Cortisol raises glucose to prepare for "threat," so chronically high cortisol keeps glucose elevated even fasted. Your fasting glucose might be 105-110 when it should be 90-95.
- Frequent illness or slow recovery from illness. Cortisol is immunosuppressive at high levels. You catch every cold that goes around. It takes you longer to recover.
- Difficulty concentrating. Cortisol activates your sympathetic nervous system, leaving you scattered rather than focused. You can't settle into deep work.
- Feeling "tired but wired"—physically exhausted but mentally restless. You're knackered but can't sleep. You're exhausted but anxious.
- Digestive issues. Cortisol shuts down digestion when it's elevated, causing bloating, poor nutrient absorption, and IBS-type symptoms.

If you have three or more of these, your cortisol is almost certainly elevated.

## Testing Cortisol

The most practical way to assess cortisol is a four-point salivary cortisol test. You collect saliva samples at four points during the day (morning upon waking, midday, evening, and before bed), and you measure the cortisol in each.

This gives you your cortisol curve. In healthy men, it should be high in the morning (waking you up), declining throughout the day, and low in the evening (allowing sleep). If it's flat (elevated all day) or inverted (low in the morning, high at night), you have dysregulation.

Medichecks, Monitor My Health, and LetsGetChecked all offer this test in the UK for roughly £60-80. It's worth doing if you're experiencing the symptoms above.

Alternatively, a single morning cortisol test via your GP or a private clinic can give you baseline data, though it's less informative than the four-point test. A fasting morning cortisol above about 450 nmol/L (roughly 16 mcg/dL in American units) might suggest elevated baseline cortisol.

Dried urine cortisol is another option, measuring 24-hour cortisol output, but it's less specific to the daily rhythm.

## The Cortisol Reduction Toolkit

If your cortisol is elevated, here's the evidence-based approach:

**Sleep is first.** We discussed this in Chapter 4. Poor sleep raises cortisol and impairs your circadian rhythm. Fix your sleep, and cortisol starts to normalise. This is the single most important intervention. Everything else is secondary to fixing sleep quality and duration. You can't supplement or exercise your way out of sleep deprivation.

**Ashwagandha.** This has the strongest evidence of any supplement for reducing cortisol. A 2012 study by Chandrasekhar found that ashwagandha supplementation reduced cortisol by 27.9% in chronically stressed adults. The standard dose is 300mg of a standardised extract (usually 5% withanolides) twice daily. It takes 4-6 weeks to work. It's not magic—you still need to address the sources of stress—but if your stress is real and you can't immediately eliminate it, ashwagandha has evidence and is worth trying. Cost is about £15-25 per month. Look for a standardised extract, not a powder that might not contain therapeutic levels.

**Aerobic exercise.** Moderate aerobic exercise—walking, cycling, swimming—activates the parasympathetic nervous system (your "rest and digest" system) and reduces acute stress. But here's the nuance: excessive aerobic exercise raises cortisol. A long run every day raises cortisol as a physical stressor on top of your mental stress. But a 30-minute brisk walk or a moderate spin on the bike? That's stress-relieving, especially if it's outside in nature. Include it, but don't overdo it. A walk is often better than a run if you're already under stress.

**Meditation and breathwork.** Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) has solid evidence for reducing cortisol. You don't need to be good at it. 10-15 minutes daily of sitting quietly, focusing on your breath, or using a guided meditation app has measurable effects on cortisol and nervous system activation. Wim Hof breathing, box breathing (4 count in, 4 count hold, 4 count out), or other deliberate breathwork also activates the parasympathetic nervous system and lowers acute cortisol. These are free or cheap and evidence-supported.

**Social connection.** This is massively underrated. Men with strong social connections have lower baseline cortisol and better stress resilience. If you're isolated, spending time with friends and family literally lowers your cortisol. Isolation is a stressor. Community is medicine. Make it a priority.

**Reducing the actual stressors.** This is the obvious one and also the hardest. If your job is genuinely unsustainable, your relationship is chronically stressful, or your life situation is unmanageable, no supplement will fix it long-term. You might need to make changes: switching jobs, addressing a relationship, simplifying your life, delegating more, saying no more. These are big decisions, but they're more effective than any cortisol-lowering supplement. A man in an unsustainable situation will have chronically elevated cortisol no matter how much ashwagandha he takes.

## Heart Rate Variability as a Practical Proxy

HRV (heart rate variability) is the variation in time between heartbeats. A higher HRV indicates better parasympathetic tone (relaxation) and lower sympathetic activation (stress). It's a useful proxy for cortisol and stress status.

Most wearables measure HRV. If you see your HRV dropping week on week, that's a sign your cortisol and stress are rising. If it's climbing, stress is declining.

You can use this practically: if your HRV drops, it's a sign to back off training, improve sleep, and manage stress. You don't need to obsess over the exact number—you're looking for the trend. A sudden dip usually means something acute happened (poor sleep, stressful day). A gradual decline means stress is building.

## What Doesn't Work

**Cortisol-blocking supplements.** Most of them are marketing nonsense. Phosphatidylserine has some evidence (a few studies showing modest cortisol reduction), but the effect size is small—maybe 10%. Most other "cortisol blockers" have no human evidence or weak evidence. Your money is better spent on sleep, ashwagandha, or a therapist.

**Adaptogens beyond ashwagandha.** Rhodiola and ginseng have some research, but ashwagandha has the strongest and most consistent evidence. Don't waste money on other adaptogens.

**Magnesium for cortisol specifically.** Magnesium is good for sleep (we discussed this), and better sleep helps cortisol normalise, but magnesium itself doesn't directly lower cortisol. It helps sleep, which indirectly helps.

## Practical Changes: The Honest Audit

Three years ago, my cortisol was genuinely elevated. I was: running a business with high stakes, trying to optimise fitness, not sleeping enough, overtraining, checking email constantly from 6am to 11pm, and not taking genuine breaks. No proper weekend—I was always "on."

My HRV was poor (high 30s when it should be mid-50s). My resting heart rate was higher than it should be (68 bpm when it should be 55-60). I had abdominal fat that wouldn't shift despite training hard. I felt "tired but wired" most of the time.

When I actually looked at it honestly, I realised: I wasn't managing stress. I was just "pushing through." So I made some changes:

- I set email boundaries. No email before 9am or after 6pm. This alone reduced constant low-grade stress. I wasn't constantly checking. The fear of missing something decreased once I created the boundary.
- I took one full day off per week where I didn't think about work. Actually off, not "working from home on Sunday." This mattered more than anything else.
- I reduced training volume (discussed in Chapter 6), which reduced overtraining stress on top of life stress. Less cortisol from training allowed my body to recover from life stress.
- I prioritised sleep ruthlessly. 7.5-8 hours, every night. I didn't negotiate with myself about this.
- I started meditating 10 minutes daily. Just sitting, breathing, nothing fancy. Often just walking meditation in the park.
- I included a 30-minute walk most days, not for cardio, but for relaxation. Often with a podcast, sometimes in silence.

I didn't supplement with ashwagandha because I didn't think I needed it once I'd addressed the actual stressors. If you genuinely can't change your stressors in the short term, ashwagandha makes sense. But the long-term fix is the stressors.

Within 4-6 weeks, my HRV was noticeably better (up to mid-50s). Within 12 weeks, my cortisol curve (I had retested) was normal again. High in the morning, declining during the day, low at night. And my testosterone improved alongside it.

I didn't change my diet or my training significantly during this period. The testosterone improvement came from cortisol normalisation.

## The Critical Point

Cortisol is often the invisible ceiling preventing testosterone optimisation. You can do everything else right and still be suppressed if your cortisol is chronically elevated.

The fix is the same hierarchy as everything else in this book: sleep first, then address actual stressors, then add supplements if needed. Most men need to reduce stress and improve sleep more than they need a supplement.

And if you're thinking "but I can't change my job" or "I can't escape my stress," understand that might be true, but it also means you're living in a way that's destroying your health. If that's your situation, the cortisol management is even more important, and you might need to have deeper conversations about your life structure. You might need to find a different job. You might need to make changes that feel big and scary. But your health is worth it.

Your body is sending you a signal. Listen to it.

# CHAPTER 8: THE EVIDENCE-BASED SUPPLEMENT STACK

Most supplement marketing is a brilliant scam. The supplement industry is worth tens of billions globally. Most of that money is spent on things with no real evidence behind them. That's not incompetence, it's deliberate: there's more money to be made from a proprietary blend that doesn't work than from a proven intervention that costs £30 a month. Proprietary blends give you plausible deniability ("it's a trade secret") while charging premium prices. Proven interventions are commoditised and cheap.

The supplement industry exploits a fundamental gap: the evidence for testosterone enhancement is genuinely thin. There aren't many interventions that consistently and dramatically raise testosterone. So the marketing fills that gap with speculation, animal studies, and stories. You get articles titled "The 5 Supplements That Skyrocketed My Testosterone" with no actual evidence, just anecdotes and photos.

This chapter is different. I'm going to tell you about three supplements that are actually worth taking (or at least worth considering), and several that you should absolutely skip. But first, let me be clear about what counts as evidence.

## What Actually Counts as Evidence

Not all evidence is equal. Here's the hierarchy:

At the bottom: cell studies and animal studies. A supplement might increase testosterone in a test tube or in rats, but that tells you almost nothing about what happens in humans. The rat physiology is different, the dosing is different, the metabolism is different. Yet supplement companies will take a single rat study and build an entire marketing campaign around it. Rats are not humans. Ignore this level of evidence entirely when evaluating supplements.

Above that: observational studies in humans. People taking supplement X seem to have higher testosterone. But this is correlation, not causation. Maybe they also sleep better, train harder, or have better genetics. You can't separate the variable.

Above that: small studies in specific populations. Maybe a study shows ashwagandha helped testosterone in 20 men with severe stress. That's better evidence, but it's a small group and a specific population. Does it apply to you? Maybe, maybe not.

At the top: randomised controlled trials (RCTs) in reasonably large populations that are relevant to you. Ideally multiple such trials showing consistent results. This is the gold standard. When I recommend something, I'm talking about this level of evidence.

Almost nothing in the supplement world has this level of evidence. This is why I'm recommending so few things.

## The Tier 1 Stack: What Actually Matters

I'm going to tell you what I actually take, and why. Not because it will magically boost testosterone, but because these interventions have legitimate evidence and fill real deficiencies in most UK men.

### Vitamin D3 + K2

Seventy to eighty percent of UK adults are deficient in vitamin D. Not barely deficient—genuinely deficient. Most of us don't get enough sun exposure (we're in the UK, the sun is weak half the year, and many of us spend 8+ hours indoors), and food sources are limited (oily fish and fortified milk, mostly).

Why does this matter for testosterone? Vitamin D is actually a steroid hormone, not a vitamin. Your cells have vitamin D receptors. Your testes have vitamin D receptors. Multiple studies have shown that low vitamin D is associated with low testosterone, and vitamin D supplementation can modestly improve testosterone, especially if you're deficient to begin with.

You don't need megadoses. The evidence supports 2000-4000 IU of D3 daily. Get tested first if you can. A 25-OH vitamin D test costs about £30 through Medichecks or Monitor My Health. Aim for a level of 100-150 nmol/L (or 40-60 ng/dL in American units). If you're below 50 nmol/L, start with 4000 IU daily. If you're between 50-100, 2000 IU is fine. If you're above 100, you probably don't need to supplement, but there's no harm in 2000 IU as maintenance.

Why K2 with it? Vitamin D increases calcium absorption. Without K2, that calcium might calcify in your arteries rather than deposit in your bones. K2 directs calcium to bone. 100-200 micrograms of MK-7 (the form of K2 with the most evidence) daily is the standard. You can get D3 and K2 together in one supplement. Cost: about £10-15 per month for a decent product.

UK brands: Myprotein, Nutri Advanced, Lamberts. Look for D3 (cholecalciferol), not D2, and MK-7 K2 specifically.

### Magnesium Glycinate

Magnesium deficiency is extremely common. Most UK men don't get enough from food. The mineral is needed for over 300 enzymatic processes in your body, including testosterone production. Several studies, notably Maggio et al. (2011), have shown a positive correlation between magnesium status and testosterone levels.

But here's the thing: magnesium is also crucial for sleep quality and stress resilience. Most people who supplement magnesium notice better sleep first, before any testosterone benefit. That's the actual value—better sleep means better testosterone indirectly.

Dosing: 300-400mg of elemental magnesium daily. Why glycinate form specifically? Most magnesium supplements use cheaper forms (oxide, citrate) that pull water into your digestive system, causing loose stools. Glycinate is bound to the amino acid glycine, which has a gentler effect on digestion and actually helps sleep (glycine on its own has evidence for improving sleep quality). It costs a bit more—about £15-20 per month—but it's worth it.

Take it in the evening, ideally with food. Some people take it before bed. Food sources include leafy greens, nuts, seeds, and dark chocolate, but you'd need to eat a lot to hit 300-400mg daily.

### Omega-3 (EPA+DHA)

Fish oil is one of the most studied supplements on the planet, and the evidence is pretty solid for cardiovascular health, anti-inflammatory effects, and mood. The testosterone evidence is less direct, but omega-3 does affect testosterone-related parameters (SHBG, inflammation, cardiovascular function).

More importantly, most UK men don't eat enough fish (particularly oily fish like mackerel, sardines, herring), so deficiency is real. And if you're not eating fish regularly, supplementing omega-3 is straightforward.

Dosing: 2 grams of combined EPA+DHA daily. That's not 2g of fish oil (which is mostly neither EPA nor DHA), it's 2g of the active compounds.

Form matters: triglyceride form is better than ethyl ester. Triglyceride is closer to how omega-3 naturally exists in fish, and it's absorbed better. Ethyl ester is cheaper, but you're paying to absorb less. Check the label.

Quality check: look for products with IFOS (International Fish Oil Standards) certification. This means they've been tested for purity and aren't loaded with heavy metals. Costs about £15-25 per month for a decent product. UK brands: Myprotein, Optimum Nutrition, Nutricia, Healthspan.

## The Tier 2 Stack: Worth Considering

These have decent evidence but are less critical than Tier 1. They're nice additions if you've nailed the foundations and have some budget.

### Creatine Monohydrate

Creatine doesn't directly affect testosterone. But it's unquestionably the most studied and effective supplement for muscle strength and performance. If you train with weights, creatine will help you get stronger and build more muscle. Better training outcomes mean better long-term testosterone support (indirectly).

Dosing: 3-5 grams daily. No loading phase needed despite what you've read. Just 5g daily and it will accumulate in your system over 3-4 weeks. Cost: about £8-10 per month. It's essentially free.

Side effects: it pulls water into muscle cells, so you need to drink a bit more water. That's it. It's not a steroid, it doesn't damage your kidneys (that's a myth—multiple studies have debunked this), and it's banned by nothing except some very conservative sports federations.

### Ashwagandha KSM-66

We discussed ashwagandha in Chapter 7 for cortisol. It has genuine evidence, particularly the KSM-66 extract (5% withanolides standardisation). The Chandrasekhar 2012 study showed 28% reduction in cortisol in chronically stressed men. The Wankhede 2015 study showed modest testosterone improvements in resistance-training men.

But here's the important bit: ashwagandha works best if you're stressed or sleep-deprived. If you're already managing stress well and sleeping 8 hours, ashwagandha might not move the needle much. It's a stress-management tool, not a testosterone booster.

Dosing: 300-600mg daily of a standardised extract. 300mg is probably enough if your stress is moderate. 600mg if stress is high. Cycle it—maybe 8 weeks on, 2 weeks off—to avoid your body habituating to it. Cost: £15-25 per month.

### Zinc

Zinc is essential for testosterone production. Deficiency suppresses testosterone. But—and this is crucial—if you're not deficient, more zinc doesn't raise testosterone. And megadosing zinc is harmful. It creates copper deficiency, which worsens liver and neurological function.

The Prasad 1996 study showed that zinc supplementation in deficient men improved testosterone. But most UK men eating reasonable amounts of protein aren't deficient. Food sources are good: beef, shellfish, pumpkin seeds, chickpeas.

Only supplement zinc if you've tested and you're genuinely deficient. If so, 15-30mg elemental zinc daily (not zinc oxide—that's poorly absorbed). Stop once your levels are normal. Cost when needed: £5-8 per month.

## What to Skip Entirely

Most testosterone supplements are a waste. Here's why and what to avoid:

**Testosterone boosters (proprietary blends).** Most contain tribulus terrestris, fenugreek, horny goat weed, or D-Aspartic Acid (DAA). The evidence is weak or non-existent. DAA had some early promising studies, then failed replication in trained men (the population that would benefit most). Proprietary blends hide the doses, making it impossible to know if you're getting an effective amount. And you're paying £40-60 per month for something that probably does nothing. Avoid entirely.

**BCAAs (branched-chain amino acids).** If you're eating adequate protein (which you should be), you're getting enough BCAAs. Supplementing extra BCAAs on top of whole food protein is redundant and expensive. Spend money on whole food or a basic whey protein instead.

**Mass gainers.** They're just expensive calories in a tub with marketing attached. Eat rice, oats, and peanut butter. It's cheaper and more nutritious.

**Most pre-workouts at full dose.** A scoop of pre-workout often contains 400+ mg of caffeine, plus beta-alanine, citrulline, and various stimulants. This is overstimulation for most people. You don't need it. A cup of strong coffee (100-200mg caffeine) before training is sufficient and costs nothing.

## Monthly Cost Breakdown

If you take the Tier 1 stack:
- Vitamin D3 + K2: £10-15
- Magnesium Glycinate: £15-20
- Omega-3: £15-25
- Total: £40-60 per month

That's reasonable. If you wanted to add Tier 2:
- Creatine: +£8-10

- Total: £60-95 per month

Still affordable. Anything more than that and you're chasing diminishing returns.

## How to Build Your Stack

Don't start everything at once. You won't be able to tell what's working, and if you have a negative reaction, you won't know what caused it.

Start with vitamin D3 (test first if you can). Take 2000-4000 IU daily for 4 weeks and see how you feel. Most people notice nothing (which is fine—it's working at a cellular level). Then add magnesium glycinate. Again, 4 weeks. Then omega-3. Stagger them.

Once you've been consistent with Tier 1 for 8-12 weeks, consider adding creatine or ashwagandha depending on your situation.

## The Real Talk

Supplements are the icing on the cake. They're not the cake. The cake is sleep, training, diet, and stress management. If you're sleeping 6 hours, not training, eating poorly, and stressed out of your mind, no supplement will meaningfully improve your testosterone. Fix those first. Then, if you want to optimise an extra 5-10%, add supplements.

Too many men spend £100+ per month on supplements while sleeping 6 hours and wondering why they feel terrible. It doesn't work that way.

# CHAPTER 9: THE ADAPTOGENS

"Adaptogen" is a loose term. It originally meant a substance that helps your body adapt to stress without side effects. But the term has been commercialised to the point that anything vaguely stress-related gets marketed as an adaptogen.

Most adaptogens don't have strong evidence for testosterone specifically. But two—ashwagandha and tongkat ali—have genuine (if modest) human data. Let me separate fact from marketing.

## What an Adaptogen Actually Is

The working definition: a substance that helps your body physiologically adapt to stress and normalise function. The key word is "normalise"—adaptogens shouldn't artificially boost a hormone that's already healthy. They should help restore balance when stress has disrupted it.

In practice, very few substances meet this definition rigorously. The supplement industry uses "adaptogen" much more loosely.

## Ashwagandha in Detail

We touched on ashwagandha in Chapter 7. Let me go deeper.

Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) is an Ayurvedic herb used for thousands of years. Modern research has focused on its bioactive compounds, particularly withanolides. The quality and evidence varies dramatically between products.

**Which ashwagandha product matters:** Not all ashwagandha is equal. You want a standardised extract with at least 5% withanolides. The two best-studied extracts are KSM-66 and Sensoril. KSM-66 has more human data, particularly for cortisol and testosterone. Sensoril has good data too, particularly for mood. Generic ashwagandha powders often contain much lower withanolide content and are essentially placebos.

**The evidence base:** The Chandrasekhar 2012 study is the primary evidence for cortisol reduction. 64 chronically stressed adults took either KSM-66 (300mg twice daily) or placebo for 60 days. The ashwagandha group saw a 27.9% reduction in cortisol and significant improvements in stress measures. This is a legitimate study with a decent sample size.

For testosterone specifically, the Wankhede 2015 study is the main evidence. 57 resistance-training men took either KSM-66 (300mg twice daily) or placebo for 8 weeks. The ashwagandha group showed modest testosterone improvements (an average increase of about 96 ng/dL from baseline). But—and this is important—the effect was modest and only measured at the end of the intervention. It's not a dramatic hormone booster.

**Sleep evidence:** Multiple studies show ashwagandha improves sleep quality, particularly in people with insomnia. This is possibly the strongest evidence.

**What ashwagandha won't do:** It won't dramatically raise testosterone in a well-nourished, well-sleeping man with low stress. It won't do anything if you're sleeping 5 hours and working 70-hour weeks. It's a stress-management tool, not a replacement for actually managing stress.

**Dosing:** 300-600mg of a standardised extract daily. 300mg is probably sufficient unless your stress is very high. Take it consistently for 4-6 weeks before judging whether it's working. You can dose it all at once or split it (150mg twice daily).

**Cycling:** Some people recommend cycling ashwagandha—8 weeks on, 2 weeks off—to prevent habituation. There's limited evidence this is necessary, but it's a reasonable approach if you want to be cautious.

## Tongkat Ali (Longjack)

Tongkat ali (Eurycoma longifolia) is a Malaysian plant traditionally used for sexual function and vitality. Modern research has focused on it for testosterone and physical performance.

**The extract that has data:** Tongkat ali products vary wildly. The extract with the most human research is Physta (standardised to 22% eurycomanone). Products with this specific extract have the best evidence base.

**The evidence:** The Tambi 2012 study is the primary evidence. It studied 76 men (aged 30-55) with confirmed hypogonadism (low testosterone, symptomatic) given either Physta extract (200mg daily) or placebo for 12 weeks. The tongkat ali group showed meaningful improvements in testosterone (from an average of 4.5 nmol/L to 8.3 nmol/L—significant for men in that range) and also improvements in sexual function and mood.

The Henkel 2014 study showed tongkat ali improved perceived stress, fatigue, and mood in men under chronic stress. It also showed modest improvements in hormonal markers.

**What this means:** Tongkat ali has real evidence, but it's specifically for men who are genuinely low-testosterone or under significant stress. It's not a booster for normal men. If you're already optimised (sleeping well, trained, fed properly), tongkat ali probably won't do much.

**Dosing:** 200-400mg of a standardised extract daily. Start with 200mg and see how you respond.

**Cycle or continuous:** Less data on cycling compared to ashwagandha, but cycling is probably fine if you want to take a break periodically.

## Rhodiola Rosea

Rhodiola has reasonable evidence for fatigue and cognitive performance under stress. It's not specifically a testosterone herb, but it's worth knowing about if you're stressed and fatigued.

The mechanism is different from ashwagandha. Rhodiola seems to improve mood and reduce fatigue through effects on dopamine and other neurotransmitters, rather than directly affecting stress hormones.

Dosing: 300-600mg of a standardised extract (3% rosavins). Cost: similar to ashwagandha (£15-25 per month).

It's genuinely useful for stressed professionals, though it's less studied than ashwagandha.

## What Adaptogens Won't Fix

This is crucial: adaptogens are not a substitute for actually addressing stress.

You cannot ashwagandha your way out of a 5-hour sleep routine. You cannot supplement your way out of a 500-calorie daily deficit. You cannot adaptogen your way out of 60-hour work weeks.

Adaptogens are optimisers for situations where your lifestyle is reasonable but stress is present. They're not a band-aid for a fundamentally broken situation.

I've seen men take ashwagandha religiously while sleeping 5 hours, overtraining, and running a business into the ground. The ashwagandha isn't going to fix that. The fix is addressing the actual stressors.

## Honest Expectations

Adaptogens are the 5-10% optimisers, not the foundation. They're worth considering once you've nailed sleep, diet, training, and have some budget left. They're not worth obsessing over.

The Chandrasekhar study showed a 28% reduction in cortisol with ashwagandha. But fixing sleep alone (say, from 6 to 8 hours) will reduce cortisol far more than 28%. The order of intervention matters. Foundations first, then adaptogens.

# CHAPTER 10: WHAT TO SKIP

The supplement industry will happily take your money for nothing. Learning to read between the lines and identify marketing nonsense is essential.

## Why the Industry is Structured to Mislead

In the UK and US, supplements fall under different regulatory frameworks than medicines. DSHEA in the US means supplements don't require pre-market proof of efficacy or safety—they just can't make disease claims. The UK has similar regulatory gaps. This creates a situation where companies can sell products with zero human evidence and market them aggressively.

A pharmaceutical company can't sell a drug without proving it works. A supplement company can sell a "testosterone booster" with no human evidence whatsoever. The playing field is asymmetrical.

Given this reality, companies optimise for profit, not truth. And the profit incentive is strongest for products with weak evidence—because weak evidence creates opportunity for marketing to fill the gap. A product with strong evidence (like vitamin D) is commoditised and cheap. A product with weak evidence can command premium pricing and strong marketing, because customers can't easily tell it doesn't work.

## Common Marketing Tricks

**"Clinically studied ingredients."** This is the oldest trick. A supplement will claim its ingredients are "clinically studied"—true, but misleading. The ingredient might have been studied at 500mg in the research, but the product contains 50mg. The dose in the product is a fraction of the studied dose, so the research doesn't apply.

**Celebrity endorsements.** A fitness influencer posts a photo with a supplement and says it changed his life. Never believe this. Influencers are paid to say this. It's marketing, not evidence. If you paid him, he'd say the same about the opposite product.

**Before and after photos.** These are meaningless. The person probably trained hard and dieted properly. The supplement had nothing to do with it. Genetics, lighting, angles, and a tan do a lot of work in those photos.

**"Proprietary blend."** This is a red flag. A proprietary blend means you don't know the dose of each ingredient. Maybe the "testosterone booster" contains 50mg of an ingredient when the study used 500mg. The company isn't telling you. Avoid proprietary blends entirely.

**"Natural" or "herbal."** This means nothing. Deadly nightshade is natural. Lead is natural. Natural doesn't mean safe or effective.

## Specific Categories to Be Sceptical Of

**Testosterone boosters:** Most contain weak evidence ingredients at weak doses. Tribulus terrestris, fenugreek, horny goat weed—these have minimal human evidence. DAA (D-Aspartic Acid) had one early promising study in infertile men, then failed replication in trained athletes (the population that would actually benefit). Most "testosterone boosters" are essentially placebos at premium prices. The whole category is a scam.

**Fat burners:** Thermogenesis (heat production) is real but tiny—maybe 5-10% of your daily calorie burn. Most fat burners are just caffeine (which is cheap) marketed as a cutting-edge thermogenic complex. You can get more caffeine more cheaply from coffee.

**Estrogen blockers (over the counter):** DIM (diindolylmethane) has modest evidence for affecting oestrogen metabolism, but that doesn't mean it dramatically lowers oestrogen in healthy men. Most men shouldn't be taking DIM. If your oestradiol is genuinely elevated, a proper AI (aromatase inhibitor) prescribed by a doctor is what you need, not a supplement.

**Collagen for muscle growth:** Collagen doesn't contain significant amounts of leucine, so it doesn't stimulate muscle protein synthesis. It might help connective tissue (cartilage, tendons) because it's collagen, but for muscle growth, whey protein is far superior.

**Most "male enhancement" supplements:** Usually contain underdosed versions of things with weak evidence, or just caffeine and some proprietary blend nonsense.

## How to Evaluate Any Supplement Yourself

Here's a simple framework:

1. Look for human RCTs, not animal studies or cell studies. What was actually tested in humans?
2. Check the dose in the study versus the dose in the product. If the product is 1/10th the studied dose, it probably won't work.
3. Check the effect size. Did testosterone go from 300 to 310 ng/dL? That's statistically significant but not clinically meaningful. Did it go from 300 to 450? That matters.
4. Check who funded the study. If the supplement company funded it, take the results with extreme scepticism. Conflict of interest is rampant.
5. Check whether the results have been replicated. One study is not enough. Multiple independent studies showing similar results is what you want.
6. Ask: is this effect size larger than what I'd get from better sleep, better diet, or better training? If not, why am I spending money on it?

## The £500/Year Question

Most men spend £50-100 per month on supplements—£600-1200 per year—on products with weak or no evidence.

If you cut that in half and actually used the money for something else, what would you buy?

A gym membership: £40-60/month. Training hard is one of the most effective interventions for testosterone.

Bloodwork: quarterly testing (4 times a year, £30-50 each): £120-200/year. Knowing your numbers actually beats guessing.

Quality whole food: £20-30/week extra for better food (grass-fed beef, wild fish, eggs, vegetables). That's £1000+ per year, which is more than most supplement budgets, and it's an actual intervention.

A therapist or counsellor: if stress is your problem, a real therapist is more effective than any adaptogen. £60-100 per session, maybe once per week: £3000-5000 per year. That's a real intervention for a real problem.

The point: before spending £1000+ per year on supplements with weak evidence, spend that money on things with strong evidence. Sleep, training, diet, and mental health come first. Supplements come later.

# CHAPTER 11: TRT IN THE UK—A STRAIGHT GUIDE

Testosterone replacement therapy (TRT) is a legitimate medical treatment for men with clinically low testosterone. It's also wildly over-prescribed to men who don't need it, and massively underutilised by men who do.

This chapter is for men considering TRT, either through the NHS or private routes. I'm not going to tell you whether to do it—that's a personal decision with a doctor. I'm going to tell you exactly what the options are in the UK, what to expect, what the evidence shows, and what the realistic outcomes are.

## Who TRT Is Actually For

TRT is indicated for men with:

1. Persistent low total testosterone (usually below 10.4 nmol/L or about 300 ng/dL) on multiple morning fasted blood tests (not just one test). The evidence of persistent low levels matters because testosterone fluctuates, and one low result doesn't mean you're hypogonadal.
2. Symptoms consistent with low testosterone: reduced libido, erectile dysfunction, fatigue, reduced wellbeing, reduced muscle mass or strength despite adequate training.
3. Secondary causes have been ruled out. Is the low testosterone due to obesity? Poor sleep? Overtraining? Untreated depression? Medication side effects? If so, the first step is addressing those, not TRT.

This is important: TRT is for men with documented hypogonadism and symptoms, not for men who "feel better" on testosterone or want performance enhancement.

## Who It's NOT For

- Men with total testosterone in the "normal" range (above 10.4 nmol/L). Even if you're at the bottom of the range and feeling suboptimal, TRT isn't appropriate unless you have symptoms and consistently low levels.
- Men who haven't addressed sleep, diet, training, and stress first. Fix those first. Many men who think they have low testosterone actually just have poor sleep and high stress.
- Men wanting to use TRT for athletic performance. That's a different conversation and outside the scope of this chapter (and legally risky).
- Men with uncontrolled hypertension or untreated sleep apnoea. TRT requires proper medical monitoring if you have cardiovascular risk factors.

## The NHS Route

Getting TRT on the NHS is possible but not straightforward.

**Criteria:** Different clinical commissioning groups (CCGs) have slightly different criteria, but generally, the NHS will consider TRT if your total testosterone is below 8-10 nmol/L with symptoms. Some areas are stricter and won't prescribe below 5-6 nmol/L. This is frustrating and variable.

**Referral pathway:** You see your GP, explain your symptoms, ask for a hormone panel (they might be reluctant), and if results are low, ask for an endocrinology or urology referral. Many GPs are uncomfortable with testosterone and might be dismissive. If your GP refuses to refer or test, you might need to push back or consider a different GP.

**Waiting times:** This varies by area, but expect 2-6 months from referral to initial consultant appointment, sometimes longer. Once approved, you'll start treatment and be monitored, but the initial diagnosis pathway is slow.

**What treatment you'll get:** The NHS typically prescribes Testogel (testosterone gel applied daily) or Nebido (a long-acting injection given every 10-14 weeks). You usually won't get injections of testosterone enanthate or cypionate (the weekly injectables), and you definitely won't get HCG or aromatase inhibitors unless there's a specific reason.

**Limitations:** The NHS won't typically prescribe HCG (which preserves fertility) or aromatase inhibitors (to manage oestradiol). If fertility is a concern, the NHS approach might not be ideal for you.

**Cost:** It's free once approved, but getting approved is the hard part.

## The Private Route

Many UK men choose private because the NHS is slow or restrictive.

**Major UK private clinics:** Optimale (online), Balance My Hormones (London-based, online consultations), Leger Clinic (private GP network), Harley Street Hormone Clinic (London). Costs vary, but expect £150-250 for an initial consultation and then £100-200+ per month ongoing (including clinic time, medication, and monitoring).

**What you'll get:** A consultation with a doctor (sometimes a GP, sometimes a private clinic specialising in hormones), bloodwork, and a treatment protocol. Private clinics are usually more flexible than the NHS and are more likely to prescribe the full toolkit (testosterone, HCG, aromatase inhibitor if needed).

**Turnaround:** Usually 2-4 weeks from booking to starting treatment. Significantly faster than the NHS.

**Protocols offered:** Most private clinics offer multiple options: testosterone injections (usually weekly enanthate or cypionate, sometimes every two weeks), Testogel, Nebido, or patches. You can often discuss which suits you best.

**Cost breakdown:** Initial consultation (£150-250), ongoing clinic fee (£50-150 per month), medication (testosterone ~£30-60 per month if injections, more if gels), monitoring blood tests (£80-150 every 3-6 months). Total: roughly £250-400 per month all-in.

## Protocols Explained

Different forms of TRT have different advantages and disadvantages.

**Testosterone enanthate or cypionate injections (weekly or every 2 weeks):** These are the same ester compounds you'd find in performance enhancement protocols, but at therapeutic doses. They're usually administered once per week (50-125mg) or every two weeks (100-250mg) depending on individual response. Weekly injections are generally smoother in terms of hormone levels.

Pros: well-studied, effective, cost-effective (injections are cheap), feedback is rapid (you know if a dose works within a few weeks).

Cons: requires injections, has peak-trough cycling (level is highest a few days after injection, lowest right before next injection), requires more frequent injections than gels.

**Testogel or other testosterone gels:** Applied daily to skin, absorbed through. Provides relatively stable hormone levels.

Pros: no injections, stable levels, easy to adjust dose (just use more or less gel).

Cons: more expensive per dose, requires daily application (compliance issue for some), sometimes causes skin irritation, absorbed inconsistently depending on application site and skin condition.

**Nebido:** Long-acting testosterone undecanoate injection, given every 10-14 weeks.

Pros: infrequent dosing (only 4 injections per year), consistent long-term levels.

Cons: very expensive (hundreds of pounds per injection), difficult to adjust dose quickly, if you have a side effect, you're stuck with it for weeks, less flexibility.

**Patches:** Testosterone patches applied daily. Less common in the UK.

Pros: non-invasive, stable levels.

Cons: expensive, skin irritation common, less commonly prescribed.

Most men on private TRT protocols use either weekly injections or Testogel. Both work, and choice is often personal preference and what your doctor recommends.

## What TRT Actually Delivers

Here's what to realistically expect:

**Libido and sexual function:** Usually improves relatively quickly, within 2-4 weeks. This is often the most noticeable benefit. Erections improve, desire returns, sexual satisfaction improves. This is consistent across studies and user reports.

**Energy and motivation:** Improves gradually, usually noticeable by 4-8 weeks, significant by 12 weeks. Men report feeling less fatigued, more willing to do things, better mood. This is real but gradual.

**Body composition:** Takes longer. Muscle improves gradually (better recovery, stronger training), fat loss is modest. Expect 3-6 months to see meaningful visual change. You're not going to gain 10kg of muscle in 3 months on TRT alone.

**Mood and cognitive function:** Some men report improved mood and clarity. Research supports this, but it's variable. Some notice it strongly, some barely at all.

**Strength:** Training harder and recovering better means you'll lift more weight and get stronger. But this is partly a training effect (you can recover better and train harder) and partly a hormone effect. Realistic expectation: 5-10% strength improvement over 3 months if you're training properly.

**Sleep quality:** Some men sleep better on TRT. This is probably mediated by improved mood and recovery, not a direct hormone effect.

What TRT won't do: it won't fix depression if the depression isn't caused by low testosterone. It won't fix relationship problems. It won't fix your career. It won't give you confidence if you're anxious by nature. It improves certain parameters, but it's not a magic fix for life.

## Testing and Monitoring

This is crucial. TRT requires regular bloodwork.

**Initial testing:** Before starting, you need:
- Total testosterone (multiple measurements, morning, fasted)
- Free testosterone or calculated free testosterone
- SHBG
- Oestradiol (E2)
- LH and FSH
- PSA (prostate specific antigen)
- Haematocrit (red blood cell count)
- Lipid panel (cholesterol, triglycerides)
- Liver function tests

This establishes your baseline.

**Ongoing monitoring:** After starting TRT, you'll typically retest at:
- 6 weeks (to check if dose is appropriate—target total testosterone usually around 15-25 nmol/L)
- 12 weeks
- Then every 3-6 months depending on stability

You're monitoring for:
- Is testosterone in target range?
- Is oestradiol stable and not too high? (usually aim for 50-200 pmol/L, but this is debated)
- Is PSA rising (potential prostate concern)?
- Is haematocrit rising (potential for polycythaemia)?
- Are lipids staying reasonable?

**The haematocrit concern:** One legitimate risk of TRT is polycythaemia—elevated red blood cell count, which thickens the blood and increases stroke/clot risk. This is why haematocrit monitoring is important. If it rises above 54%, you're typically advised to reduce dose, stop temporarily, or donate blood.

Most men don't develop this, but it's a real concern and requires monitoring.

## Aromatase Inhibitors (AIs)

On TRT, some testosterone converts to oestradiol (through the aromatase enzyme). Whether to manage this with an aromatase inhibitor is debated.

**The case for not using AI:** If your oestradiol stays in the 50-200 pmol/L range naturally on TRT, you probably don't need an AI. Some oestradiol is healthy (bone health, mood, joint health). "Killing your E2" is bad advice.

**The case for using AI:** If your oestradiol climbs above 200-250 pmol/L, you might have oestrogen-related side effects: water retention, breast tenderness, mood instability, sexual dysfunction. In these cases, a small dose of an AI (usually anastrozole 0.5-1mg weekly) can bring it down.

Most private clinics will measure oestradiol and decide whether AI is needed. Most don't prescribe it unless needed.

## Fertility and TRT

This is important for younger men or men wanting children.

TRT suppresses LH and FSH, which suppresses spermatogenesis (sperm production). This doesn't mean permanent infertility—it usually reverses within months of stopping—but it does mean you can't father children while on TRT without taking additional steps.

**If you want to preserve fertility on TRT:** Use HCG (human chorionic gonadotropin) alongside testosterone. HCG stimulates LH, which preserves sperm production. This is what private clinics offer, but rarely available on NHS.

Typical protocol: testosterone + HCG 250 IU three times per week. This maintains normal sperm production while you're on TRT.

**If you want children before starting TRT:** Consider banking sperm before starting, just in case.

**If you're past child-bearing years:** This isn't a concern.

## The Commitment

This is crucial to understand: for most men who respond well to TRT, it's a lifelong commitment.

Stopping TRT means your testosterone returns to baseline (low) within weeks. Your symptom improvement reverses. If you feel dramatically better on TRT, you're probably going to want to stay on it indefinitely.

This isn't necessarily a problem—if it improves your life and you're being monitored properly, long-term TRT is safe—but you need to understand the commitment upfront. You're not doing a 12-week course and then stopping. You're committing to lifelong treatment with ongoing monitoring.

## Privacy and Data

Where is this information stored? Who knows?

If you use the NHS, your TRT is on your NHS record. Your GP knows. It's confidential, but it's in the system.

If you use a private clinic, your information is with that clinic. Different clinics have different privacy policies. This is actually one reason some men prefer private—they're not in the government system, and they have more control over their information.

If you use an online clinic, data might be stored online. You should ask about their data protection and privacy policies.

## Adam's Honest Position

I've never needed TRT. My testosterone is fine. But if my bloodwork came back showing persistent hypogonadism with symptoms, I would pursue it, probably through a private clinic (for speed and flexibility). The evidence shows it works. The risk profile at proper monitoring is reasonable.

I'd probably start with weekly testosterone enanthate injections (easier to adjust than gels, clearer dose-response). I'd get tested every 6-8 weeks until stable, then quarterly.

If I had fertility concerns, I'd add HCG.

If my oestradiol rose above a reasonable level, I'd add a small AI.

I'd treat it as what it is: a medication that improves my life, not a character flaw or something to be ashamed of, and also not something to take lightly.

# CHAPTER 12: ADVANCED OPTIONS—PEPTIDES AND BEYOND

This chapter is for men who've genuinely done everything in the previous chapters and want to go further. It's not for beginners. It's not medical advice. Consider this educational information for informed, risk-tolerant individuals.

Before I continue: these are research peptides, not licensed medicines in the UK. The legal status is grey. I'm educating, not prescribing. Consult a medical professional if you're considering any of this.

## CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin Stack

This is the most common "advanced" peptide stack in the fitness world.

**What they are:** CJC-1295 is a GHRH analogue (growth hormone-releasing hormone). It tells your pituitary to release more growth hormone. Ipamorelin is a GHRP (growth hormone-releasing peptide)—a selective secretagogue that also stimulates GH release, but without as much stimulation of cortisol and prolactin compared to other GRPs.

**Why they're stacked:** GHRH and GHRP work through slightly different mechanisms. Stacking them gives a synergistic effect—more GH release than either alone.

**The evidence:** GH elevation is confirmed in human studies. Downstream benefits (body composition, recovery, performance) are strongly evidenced in preclinical models but primarily user-reported in humans. We know the peptides elevate GH. We know elevated GH (from other causes, like exercise or sleep) has benefits. But human studies specifically testing CJC + ipamorelin for fat loss or muscle gain are limited.

That said, the community evidence (thousands of users reporting effects) is consistent: improved recovery, better fat loss, improved skin quality, better sleep, improved mood. These align with what GH elevation should do.

**Typical protocol:** 200-300 mcg ipamorelin + an equivalent dose of CJC-1295 (without DAC—the DAC version is longer-acting and has less community data), administered via subcutaneous injection, usually 5 nights per week, before sleep.

Cycle: typically 12-16 weeks on, 4 weeks off.

**Cost:** About £100-200 per month for peptides (prices vary). You'll need insulin syringes (cheap), and if you're careful, you can source from verified suppliers. But there are quality and legal concerns.

**UK legal status:** These are research peptides. They're not licensed for human use. They're sold as "for research only, not for human consumption," which is a legal grey area. The MHRA hasn't explicitly banned them, but they're not regulated. Using them is a personal choice with unknown legal risk.

## Sermorelin vs CJC-1295

Sermorelin is an older GHRH analogue, shorter-acting, cleared from the body in about 10 minutes. CJC-1295 (particularly the non-DAC version) lasts a few hours. CJC-1295 without DAC has more community data and is generally preferred.

Sermorelin is rarely used now because it requires frequent injections. CJC-1295 is simpler (once daily, usually).

## BPC-157

BPC-157 (Body Protective Compound 157) is a synthetic peptide derived from gastric juice.

**What it does:** In animal models, it accelerates healing of various tissues (gut, muscle, tendon) and has anti-inflammatory properties. Limited human data, but what exists is promising for gut healing and injury recovery.

**Evidence:** Most evidence is preclinical (animals). Human studies are sparse. But what human data exists suggests benefits for gut lining integrity, which can help with nutrient absorption and digestive issues.

**Common uses:** Injury recovery (especially tendon and muscle), gut health (for leaky gut or IBS-type symptoms), general recovery.

**Dosing:** 200-600 mcg daily, usually via injection or sometimes oral (less evidence for oral efficacy).

**Cost:** Similar to other peptides, £80-150 per month.

**The honesty check:** BPC-157 is promising but under-studied in humans. Most people reporting benefits are biohackers and fitness enthusiasts, not subjects in controlled trials. It might work. It might not. The risk profile is probably low (it's derived from something your body already makes), but long-term safety data in humans is absent.

## Enclomiphene

Enclomiphene is the active isomer of clomiphene (Clomid). It's a SERM (selective oestrogen receptor modulator).

**How it works:** Enclomiphene blocks oestrogen signalling in the hypothalamus and pituitary, which stimulates LH and FSH, which stimulates natural testosterone production. This is different from TRT—you're stimulating your own production rather than replacing testosterone.

**Evidence:** The Tambi 2012 study showed clomiphene improved testosterone in hypogonadal men. Smaller studies on enclomiphene specifically show similar effects. The evidence is decent for hypogonadal men, weaker for normal men.

**Advantages:** You maintain natural testosterone production, which means preserved fertility, and you maintain normal hormonal function (LH and FSH remain active).

**Disadvantages:** Slower to act than TRT, less studied specifically, side effects include mood changes, visual disturbance (rare), headache.

**Dosing:** 12.5-25mg daily, typically.

**UK prescribing:** Enclomiphene is unlicensed in the UK, but some private clinics will prescribe it off-label for men wanting TRT without suppressing fertility.

**The honesty check:** Enclomiphene is a legitimate medical option, but it's less studied than TRT and slower-acting. It's a reasonable choice for men wanting to preserve fertility, but it's not "better" than TRT—just different.

## The Honesty Check: Peptides and Advanced Options

Here's the reality: most peptide evidence in humans is weak or absent. Most of the confidence in peptide protocols comes from community reports, not clinical trials.

This doesn't mean they don't work. It means we don't have the same level of evidence as we do for sleep, diet, or training.

These are for informed, risk-tolerant individuals who:
1. Have already optimised sleep, diet, training, and stress
2. Have genuinely low testosterone despite optimisation (or want to optimise a normal level further)
3. Accept the risks: legal grey areas, limited safety data in humans, quality control issues with sourcing
4. Are working with someone knowledgeable (ideally a medical professional)
5. Understand they're experimenting, not following a proven protocol

If you're sleeping poorly, not training, and eating badly, peptides won't fix your life. Fix the foundations first.

## What These Won't Replace

This is the critical point. Advanced pharmacological options are not a substitute for the fundamentals.

No amount of CJC-1295 will overcome poor sleep. No amount of BPC-157 will fix a terrible diet. No amount of enclomiphene will compensate for lack of training.

The hierarchy remains:
1. Sleep
2. Diet
3. Training
4. Stress management
5. Then supplements
6. Then TRT (if indicated)
7. Then advanced options (if you've done everything above and want to push further)

Most men should never need to go beyond step 5. Many should never go beyond step 3.

If you find yourself considering peptides because your sleep is bad or your diet is poor, that's a sign to fix the foundations, not to add more complexity.

## Finding a Knowledgeable Practitioner

If you're seriously considering any of this, find someone who knows what they're doing. This means:

- A doctor or clinic with experience managing TRT/peptides
- Someone who monitors bloodwork regularly (every 6-12 weeks minimum)
- Someone who adjusts protocols based on data, not dogma
- Someone who's honest about unknowns and limits

A good practitioner will tell you the limits of the evidence. A bad one will promise results and oversimplify.

Private clinics in the UK increasingly offer peptide protocols alongside TRT. This is actually good because they can monitor you and integrate it into a broader health picture. Self-sourcing peptides without medical oversight is significantly riskier.

# PART 6: PUTTING IT TOGETHER (FINAL SECTION)

## CHAPTER 13: YOUR 12-WEEK PROTOCOL

You've read the theory. You understand your hormones. You know what's available. Now comes the part that actually matters: doing the work.

This chapter is a practical framework for the next twelve weeks. It's not a rigid programme. It's a structure you'll adapt to your life—because protocols that don't fit into your actual existence are useless, and we're not interested in useless.

The core argument of this chapter is simple: twelve weeks is enough time to see meaningful changes if you execute the fundamentals consistently. It's long enough to spot patterns and collect real data. It's short enough that it feels achievable. If you can't commit to twelve weeks of consistent effort, you're not ready for this work yet.

### Why Twelve Weeks?

Why not eight? Or sixteen? Or just "do it forever"?

Twelve weeks is the sweet spot for several reasons. First, it's the minimum amount of time to see meaningful bloodwork changes from lifestyle and supplementation alone. A 2019 study in the Journal of Sports Medicine showed that consistent sleep improvement, training, and nutritional changes produce detectable hormonal shifts in 10-12 weeks. Your testosterone won't dramatically change, but markers like cortisol, insulin sensitivity, and inflammation will improve significantly.

Second, twelve weeks is long enough to establish habits. James Clear's research on habit formation suggests that behavioural changes become automatic around 8-10 weeks if you're consistent. By week twelve, sleeping better won't feel like a chore. Training three times a week will feel normal. You'll have real data about whether these things work for you.

Third, twelve weeks lets you do a proper retest. You get a baseline, you execute for twelve weeks, you retest under the same conditions, and you compare apples to apples. This is important: you'll actually know whether something moved, rather than guessing based on how you feel.

Finally, twelve weeks is realistic for most men. You're not committing to a year-long transformation. You're committing to a focused quarter. That's manageable. After twelve weeks, you reassess and decide whether to continue, escalate, or try something different.

### The Protocol Structure: Four Phases

The twelve weeks break down into four distinct phases. Each phase has different priorities, different focus points, and different success metrics.

#### **Phase 1: Weeks 1-2 Baseline and Audit**

This phase is about measurement and honesty. You're not changing anything yet. You're just observing.

**Bloodwork: Get tested.** This is the foundation. You need to know your actual numbers before you make any changes. I recommend Medichecks' Advanced Male Hormone Panel or Monitor My Health's equivalent. Get the full panel: total testosterone, free testosterone, SHBG, oestradiol, LH, FSH, prolactin, cortisol, thyroid (TSH, free T4), and a lipid panel.

Test in the morning, fasted. Don't train that morning—activity affects cortisol and testosterone. If you're getting NHS testing, book it at your GP surgery and have the same discussion: you want the full panel, not just total testosterone.

Cost: Private testing is roughly £150-200. NHS is free, but you might need to push for the full panel.

**Sleep audit: Track seven nights.** Download a sleep app if you have a decent smartwatch (Oura, WHOOP, Garmin), or just be honest in a note on your phone. Track: bedtime, wake time, total sleep, how you feel on waking (alert, groggy, normal).

Don't try to improve sleep yet. You're just collecting baseline data. This is important: your honest baseline is vastly more valuable than the baseline you wish you had. If you're getting five hours and pretending it's six, you're cheating.

**Diet audit: Food diary for three days.** Three days is enough to spot patterns. Two of those days should be normal work days; one should be a weekend or relaxed day.

Log everything: meals, snacks, drinks. Use MyFitnessPal if you want macros, or just write it down. Again: you're not changing anything. You're observing. The goal is to catch how you actually eat versus how you think you eat.

**Training audit: What's your current pattern?** How many days per week? What type of training? How long per session? What's the intensity feel like? Are you progressing in lifts or just doing the same thing?

Write this down. This is your baseline.

**Stress audit: Honest assessment.** What's stressing you? Work stress, financial stress, relationship stress, health anxiety? What consumes mental energy? What wakes you up at 3am?

Rate your overall stress level 1-10. Rate your sense of control 1-10. You're building a picture of your psychological load.

**Baseline self-assessment.** Rate yourself on 1-10 scales for:
- Energy (1 = exhausted, 10 = wired)
- Libido (1 = non-existent, 10 = constantly horny)
- Mood (1 = depressed, 10 = euphoric; aim for neutral-to-good, so 6-7 is baseline)
- Physical performance (1 = can barely move, 10 = peak athletic ability)
- Body satisfaction (1 = hate it, 10 = love it)

Write all this down. Photos are optional but useful—front, side profile, same time of day, same lighting. You don't need to share these with anyone. They're for you.

At the end of week two, you have: bloodwork, sleep data, dietary pattern, training pattern, stress assessment, and baseline scores.

#### **Phase 2: Weeks 3-6 Foundations First**

Now you implement the non-negotiables. Not all at once. Sequentially. This prevents overwhelm and lets you see what actually matters.

**Sleep: The non-negotiables.**

The evidence for sleep is so strong that this gets priority. You can't supplement your way past bad sleep. You can't train around poor sleep. You can't diet your way past it.

Non-negotiable changes:
- Bedroom temperature: 18-19°C (or as close as you can get). This is the evidence-based optimal. If you can't cool your room that much, aim for 18°C minimum.
- Consistent wake time: Pick a time and stick to it, seven days a week. Yes, weekends. Circadian rhythm consistency matters more than sleep duration. If you're waking at 6:30am on weekdays and 10am on weekends, your hormones are confused.
- No alcohol within three hours of sleep. Alcohol fragments sleep architecture, especially REM and deep sleep. None for three hours before bed.
- Screens down 60 minutes before bed. The blue light is real. The wind-down time is real. Non-negotiable.
- Dark room. Blackout curtains if needed.

Implement these all in week three. They're not optional.

Cost: Blackout curtains (Amazon, £20-50). Temperature control might mean a fan or an AC unit, or just opening windows. Most of this costs nothing.

**Supplementation: Tier 1 only.**

Not a stack. Just three things:
- Vitamin D3: 2,000-4,000 IU daily. UK sun exposure is inadequate for most people. This is deficiency correction, not supplementation. Take with a meal containing fat for absorption.
- Magnesium Glycinate: 300mg daily, before bed. This helps with sleep quality and appears to support testosterone production. Glycinate form is easier on the gut than oxide.
- Omega-3 fish oil: 2g EPA/DHA daily. Cardiovascular health, hormonal health, cognitive support. Standard dosing.

That's it. Not ZMA, not tribulus, not ashwagandha, not "advanced hormone boosters." Those come later, if at all.

Cost: About £25-35 per month from Bulk Powders, Myprotein, or NSP.

**Training: If you're not lifting, start simple.**

If you're already lifting three or more days per week with progressive overload, skip this. If not, start now.

A simple three-day full-body programme takes 45 minutes and requires a gym or basic home setup:

Day 1: Squat, bench press, row (5x5 each, or 3x8-12 if you prefer)
Day 2: Deadlift, overhead press, lat pulldown (3x5 or 3x8-12)
Day 3: Squat variation, incline bench, barbell row (3x8-12)

Rest days in between. Progressive overload: add weight or reps each week.

You're not trying to become a bodybuilder. You're creating stimulus for testosterone and muscle retention. Three days a week, decent effort, basic movements. This is proven.

The standard resources for this are strong: StrongLifts 5x5, Starting Strength (Rippetoe's book), or any sensible strength template. I'm not designing a programme in this book because good ones already exist.

**Diet: Three changes, not a overhaul.**

Not a diet. Three non-negotiable changes:

1. Adequate protein: 0.7-1g per pound of bodyweight daily. For a 200lb man, that's 140-200g protein. This is necessary for hormonal function and muscle maintenance. Hit it consistently.

2. Reduce ultra-processed food. Not eliminate. Just reduce. Fast food, takeaways, processed snacks—aim for three or fewer instances per week instead of daily. This has second-order effects on blood sugar, inflammation, and sleep quality.

3. Alcohol audit. If you're drinking more than 14 units per week (UK guidance), reduce to 14 or below. More than that suppresses testosterone and disrupts sleep. Track it honestly.

Do these three things. Don't start a strict diet. Don't obsess about macros. Just do these three things for four weeks and see what happens.

**Stress: Identify and address two chronic stressors.**

Not all stress. Pick the two biggest controllable stressors in your life.

Work stress? Propose a specific change: reduced emails after 6pm, or a conversation with your boss about workload.
Financial stress? Have a specific conversation or take one concrete action.
Relationship stress? Suggest one date or conversation.
Health anxiety? Schedule a check-up that's been pending.

Don't try to "fix" your life. Make two specific, concrete changes that address actual stressors. Track how you feel.

**Phase 2 summary:** By the end of week six, you're sleeping better, supplementing the basics, training consistently, eating more protein, drinking less alcohol, and you've addressed two actual stressors.

You haven't changed your hormones dramatically yet. But you've changed your context. You've created the environment where hormones can optimize.

#### **Phase 3: Weeks 7-10 Add Optimisers**

Now, only if the foundations are solid, you layer in the next tier.

**If sleeping well:** Add Ashwagandha KSM-66 300mg daily. The evidence for ashwagandha on stress and cortisol is solid (Pratte 2014). But it only works if your sleep is already decent. Don't add it on top of bad sleep.

**If training consistently:** Add Creatine Monohydrate 3g daily. Cheap, safe, proven. It takes 3-4 weeks to build up in your system, but the evidence for muscle retention and recovery is strong.

**If stress remains high:** Consider HRV tracking (Heart Rate Variability via Oura or WHOOP). This gives you objective data on nervous system recovery. It helps you decide whether to push hard or dial back training. It's not essential, but it's useful if you're still managing significant stress.

**If libido or energy are still low** despite solid sleep and training: This is when you look back at your Phase 1 bloodwork. Are your testosterone, vitamin D, or other markers genuinely low? If yes, and you've done everything above for six weeks with no improvement, it's worth considering:
- A private consultation with a hormone specialist
- Potential supplementation adjustment (maybe zinc or additional magnesium dosing)
- Honest assessment of whether there are other issues (depression, relationship problems, work dissatisfaction) that aren't hormonal

But don't jump to TRT at this point. You're only at week seven. You need to know whether the problem is actually hormonal.

**Phase 3 summary:** You're now six weeks into better habits, and you're adding a second-tier supplement if it makes sense. You're collecting more data. You're checking in on how you actually feel.

#### **Phase 4: Weeks 11-12 Retest and Review**

**Bloodwork: Retest under the same conditions as baseline.**

Morning, fasted, no training that day. Same panel as week one-two.

Cost: Another £150-200, or NHS if you're going that route.

**Self-assessment: Re-score yourself on the same 1-10 scales.**

Energy, libido, mood, physical performance, body satisfaction. Compare to week one-two baseline.

**Photo comparison:** If you took photos in week one-two, take them again in the same style. Same location, same time of day, same lighting. You'll be surprised what you can see in twelve weeks.

**Analysis: What moved?**

This is the crucial conversation you have with yourself.

Your energy improved, but your testosterone barely moved? Sleep and training were the answer. Testosterone wasn't your bottleneck.

Your testosterone improved by 150 ng/dL but you don't feel that much better? Could indicate that your baseline wasn't the issue, or that other things (stress, sleep debt, mood) are still the limiting factor.

Your libido improved significantly but your bloodwork looks almost the same? Again, it might not have been a hormonal problem.

Your subjective scores improved across the board and your bloodwork shows meaningful improvements in testosterone, cortisol, inflammation markers, and insulin sensitivity? You've got your answer: the fundamentals worked.

**The Decision Point**

Now you make a decision based on data:

**Scenario 1: Significant improvement in both bloodwork and subjective symptoms.**

This is success. Your fundamentals worked. The action: continue the protocol. Don't stop. Make this your lifestyle, not a twelve-week experiment. Retest annually. Maintain the sleep, training, diet, and stress management. You might add more advanced supplementation later (like tongkat ali or more targeted adaptogenic work), but you don't need TRT.

**Scenario 2: Bloodwork improved, but subjective symptoms haven't changed much.**

This is interesting. It suggests the hormonal changes weren't your bottleneck. Your energy, mood, and libido are probably limited by something else: undiagnosed depression, relationship problems, work stress, underlying health conditions, or simple lifestyle adjustment.

Action: talk to someone (a GP, a therapist, a coach) about what might actually be limiting you. Testosterone might not be the answer.

**Scenario 3: Subjective improvement but bloodwork barely changed.**

This is also valuable data. It suggests placebo, habit benefit, or that your numbers were never the issue. Sleep improvement, training consistency, reduced stress—these create subjective wellbeing independent of testosterone.

Action: great. Keep doing what you're doing. You've found what works for you.

**Scenario 4: No improvement in either bloodwork or symptoms despite consistent effort.**

This is the concerning scenario, and it's the one that warrants escalation to medical investigation.

If you've genuinely done the fundamentals for twelve weeks and nothing's moved, you have a few options:
- Retesting by a different lab to verify the numbers
- Investigation into other causes (thyroid dysfunction, vitamin B12 deficiency, sleep apnea, depression)
- Consultation with a private hormone specialist if your GP isn't finding answers
- If bloodwork shows genuinely low testosterone (total <350 ng/dL or free <50 pg/mL) despite excellent sleep and training, TRT becomes a legitimate option

This is the point where you escalate. Not before twelve weeks. Not without the data. But if you've done the work and nothing's moved, you have a medical issue, not a lifestyle issue, and you need professional help.

### The Honest Expectation

Let me be frank about what this protocol probably won't do:

If you weren't deficient in vitamin D, magnesium, or zinc, your testosterone probably won't increase dramatically. Your numbers might improve by 5-10%, which is real but not transformative. That's because those deficiencies were never your problem.

If you were already sleeping well and training consistently, you won't see massive changes. You're already optimized. Small improvements at the margin are still good, but don't expect doubling your testosterone in twelve weeks.

If your testosterone is actually in the normal range and your symptoms are driven by depression, relationship problems, or work stress, optimizing your hormones won't fix your life. You need to address the actual problem.

But here's what will probably improve: your sleep quality will increase. Your training recovery will improve. Your energy in the afternoon will improve. Your body composition will shift slightly (less body fat, more muscle preservation if you're training). Your mood will improve. Your libido might improve. Your blood pressure and inflammation markers will improve.

This isn't magic. It's the natural result of sleeping better, moving your body consistently, eating adequately, managing stress, and supplementing actual deficiencies.

For many men, this is enough. You don't need to triple your testosterone to feel dramatically better. You just need to feel like yourself again. That's what this protocol is for.

## CHAPTER 14: MONITORING AND THE LONG GAME

So you've done your twelve weeks. You've reassessed. You know whether the fundamentals moved the needle for you. Now what?

This chapter is about sustainability. Because a protocol that works for three months but fails after three years isn't a solution—it's just a cycle of dieting and relapse.

Hormone optimization is a practice. It's not a destination. You don't reach it and declare victory and stop. You reach a baseline that works, and then you maintain it while life throws complications at you (illness, stress, aging, changing job, moving house) and you adjust.

### Ongoing Bloodwork: The Cadence

This is the first question: how often should you retest?

If you're doing lifestyle optimization alone (sleep, diet, training, supplements, no TRT):

**If you've found something that works:** Test once a year. Pick the same season, same time of day, same conditions. You want to spot trends over time: is your testosterone slowly declining, staying stable, or improving? Are inflammatory markers stable? Is vitamin D holding steady?

Once yearly is enough. More frequent testing is obsessive and doesn't give you better information.

**If you're experimenting with different protocols:** Test every twelve weeks. New supplement stack? Retest in twelve weeks to see if it moved anything. New training protocol? Twelve weeks, then retest.

**If you're on TRT:**

This is different. TRT requires monitoring. You want to know:
- Is your testosterone stable at the target level?
- How's your oestradiol responding? (Too high or too low, both are problems.)
- What's happening to your haematocrit? (TRT can increase red blood cell production, which is good to a point but can become problematic.)
- How's your lipid profile? (Testosterone can affect cholesterol.)
- PSA and liver function, periodically?

Retest every 6-8 weeks initially (especially in the first 6 months while you're finding the right dose). Once stable, you can move to every 12 weeks. Once you've been on a stable dose for a year or more and everything's fine, you can move to annual rechecks.

But don't skip it. TRT requires monitoring.

### What to Track Between Tests

You won't retest bloodwork that often. But you can track subjective markers that matter.

The best subjective markers are:
- Morning energy levels (1-10 scale, daily for one week each month)
- Libido (frequency of desire, quality of sexual experience, monthly check-in)
- Gym performance (can you lift the same weight for more reps? Did your lifts stagnate?)
- Mood (any noticeable changes?)
- Sleep quality (are you sleeping well most nights?)
- Body composition (taking photos monthly or every two months; weighing yourself weekly if that's motivating, or monthly if you don't like frequent updates)

Track these things. Not obsessively (not every day for everything). But monthly snapshots. This gives you early warning if something's shifting.

If your energy suddenly drops despite good sleep, that's worth investigating. If your libido tanks for a month, that could be stress, could be sleep issues, could be hormonal. If your lifts suddenly feel harder, you might be overtraining or undereating or sick.

These soft markers often signal hormonal or health changes before bloodwork tells you about them.

### Spotting Patterns: Are You Improving or Stuck?

Three months of tracking will tell you a story.

Scenario 1: Things are improving or stable month to month.

Great. You know what's working. Don't change it. Stay consistent.

Scenario 2: You notice a pattern—for example, energy dips in December, picks up in March.

This is seasonal variation, which is real. Testosterone does vary seasonally (slightly lower in summer, slightly higher in autumn). Vitamin D is seasonal. Mood is seasonal. If you notice patterns, you can work with them: higher vitamin D in summer to compensate for seasonal increase in synthesis, more aggressive stress management in high-stress seasons, awareness that this is temporary.

Scenario 3: You're stuck. Energy isn't improving. Libido isn't improving. Gym performance plateaued. Nothing's shifted in two months despite doing everything you're supposed to.

This could mean:
- You need more time (some things take 4-6 months to show)
- You're not actually executing as consistently as you think (honest assessment: are you really sleeping that well?)
- The protocol isn't right for you (maybe you need different supplementation, or TRT, or investigation into other causes)
- Something else has changed (new stress, sleep apnea developed, depression, new medication)

The action: either commit to another month of strict execution and reassess, or test again and investigate why nothing's moving.

### When Things Change: Illness, Stress, Life Events

Hormones don't exist in a vacuum. Your life does things to them.

**Major illness:** Infection, surgery, or serious illness will suppress testosterone and cortisol patterns will spike. This is normal. Let your body recover. Don't be alarmed if you feel worse temporarily. Once you're well, hormones usually rebalance within weeks to a couple of months.

**Major stress event:** Job loss, relationship breakup, significant financial stress—these will suppress testosterone and raise cortisol. This is why stress management matters. But also recognize: if you're going through something major, your hormones are temporarily going to be off. Don't panic. Do extra self-care, sleep better if you can, and wait it out.

**Significant weight change:** Gained or lost 20+ pounds? This will affect your hormone levels. SHBG will change. Oestradiol will change. Give yourself six weeks to stabilize before you panic about new symptoms.

**New medication:** Some medications affect hormones. If your GP prescribes something, ask if it affects testosterone or thyroid. If it does and you're worried, ask about alternatives or timing.

**Major life transition:** New job, new relationship, moving house—these are real stressors. Your hormones might dip temporarily. This is normal. Expect it, manage it, and move on.

The point: recognize that you're not trying to maintain perfect hormones in a vacuum. Life happens. Your protocol adapts to life, not the other way around.

### The Age Progression

You're 40 now. What happens in your 50s?

Testosterone naturally declines about 1% per year after age 30 for most men. By 50, you're looking at potentially 10-15% lower than you were at 40, all else equal.

If you were at 600 ng/dL at 40, you might naturally be at 520 ng/dL at 50 without any interventions. This is normal aging.

Your response:
- Sleep becomes more fragmented. You'll need to double down on sleep hygiene.
- Recovery from training takes longer. You might need to train 2-3 times per week instead of 4-5, with more emphasis on quality than volume.
- Diet becomes more important. You can't get away with poor eating choices as easily.
- Supplementation might need adjustment. Vitamin D deficiency becomes more common with age. Magnesium becomes more important for sleep.

If you've been maintaining good habits since your 40s, you'll actually feel better in your 50s than a guy who ignored it, because at least you know what works for you.

Some men will reach a point in their 50s where TRT becomes appealing. Not because anything's broken, but because they're choosing to optimize beyond the natural decline. This is a personal choice. It's not medically necessary, but some men prefer to maintain testosterone levels similar to their 40s. That's valid.

Others will be fine with the natural decline and the adjustments it requires.

The point: keep monitoring. Keep adjusting. Expect some changes. This is aging, not failure.

### The Lifestyle Sustainability Question

Here's the question nobody asks but everyone should: can you actually maintain this for thirty years?

If your protocol requires:
- Sleeping 8.5 hours every single night
- Training an hour a day
- Meditating 20 minutes daily
- Eating perfectly
- Never going out with friends

...you can't maintain this. You'll do it for twelve weeks, maybe even a year, then life will happen and you'll burn out.

Your protocol needs to be:
- Protective sleep (7-8 hours on average, sometimes less, but protecting sleep as a priority)
- Regular training (3-4 times per week, 30-60 minutes, no perfection required)
- Basic stress management (breathing work, maybe meditation, probably just having friends and doing things you enjoy)
- Generally good eating (80/20 rule: protein and vegetables most of the time, flexibility the rest)
- Supplementation that's simple enough to maintain (four pills a day is easy; twelve supplements is not)

This version is sustainable. You can do this for thirty years.

The hard version? You'll do it until something in your life changes, then you'll quit, then you'll feel worse, then you'll feel guilty, then you'll cycle.

Design for sustainability from the start. This is not a temporary protocol. This is how you live.

### Tracking Without Obsessing

There's a line between data-informed decision making and hypochondria. You want to be on one side of that line.

Useful tracking:
- One weight measurement per week (same day, same time)
- Energy and libido check-in monthly
- Bloodwork once yearly (or as indicated by your protocol)
- Training performance (are you progressing?)
- Sleep quality (generally, not tracking every night)

Obsessive tracking:
- Daily weight measurement (normal variation is 2-3kg day to day; meaningless)
- Checking bloodwork every month
- Daily mood scoring (too much variance, hard to see patterns)
- HRV obsession (useful data, but not if you're changing your life based on daily fluctuations)
- Daily hormonal symptom tracking (you'll see patterns that aren't there)

Track enough to know what's working. Don't track so much that you lose perspective.

### Signs You Need to Escalate

Most of the time, you're managing your health with sleep, training, diet, and supplementation. But some things warrant medical investigation.

**Severe persistent fatigue despite optimal sleep, diet, training, and stress management.** This could indicate thyroid dysfunction, sleep apnea, depression, or another medical condition. Get investigated by a doctor.

**Persistent sexual dysfunction despite optimal hormones.** Could be vascular, could be psychological, could be medication-related. Needs investigation.

**Very low LH and FSH along with low testosterone.** This suggests a pituitary problem (secondary hypogonadism) rather than testicular failure. Needs endocrinology investigation.

**Signs of hyperprolactinaemia** (excessive lactation, persistent erectile dysfunction, low libido despite normal testosterone). Prolactin is controlled by the pituitary. If it's elevated, you need imaging to rule out a prolactin-secreting tumour (usually benign, but needs to be ruled out).

**Thyroid dysfunction symptoms despite normal TSH.** If you feel hypothyroid (fatigue, cold sensitivity, hair loss, slow metabolism) but your TSH is normal, you might need free T3 and T4 testing. Some people do better with specific thyroid hormone replacement even with "normal" TSH.

**Unexplained weight gain or loss.** If you're gaining weight despite proper eating and training, or losing weight without trying, something's off. Could be thyroid, could be other medical issues. Investigate.

These aren't hormone optimization questions. These are medical questions. Get professional help.

## How to Prepare for a Test

**Timing:**
- Book for early morning (6-9am ideally)
- Fasted: nothing to eat for 10-12 hours before
- No fluids except water for 2 hours before

**Lifestyle considerations:**
- Don't train the morning of your test (exercise affects testosterone and cortisol)
- Avoid alcohol the night before
- Sleep normally (not sleep-deprived or oversleep)
- Minimize stress that morning if possible
- Consistent timing: if you test on a Monday morning, retest on a Monday morning 12 weeks later

**Why:** Testosterone has a circadian rhythm (peaks in early morning) and can fluctuate day-to-day. Consistent conditions let you compare apples to apples.

## How to Read a Medichecks Report

When you get your results back, they'll look like this:

**Total Testosterone: 450 ng/dL**
Reference range: 300-900 ng/dL
Status: "Normal"

The reference range is the population normal. You're in range, so it says "normal." But 450 is in the lower third of normal. You need to:
1. Check your free testosterone (if it's proportionally low, you have a free T problem despite normal total)
2. Check your SHBG (if it's high, that explains why your free T is low)
3. Compare to previous tests if you have them (did you decline? Stay stable?)
4. Note your symptoms (do you feel low-energy despite "normal" testosterone?)

For oestradiol:
- Too low (<15): joint pain, ED, mood problems
- Just right (20-40): feel good
- Too high (>50): water retention, mood changes, potential gynecomastia

For cortisol (fasting morning):
- High (>25): chronic stress, sleep problems
- Normal (10-20): good stress management
- Low (<5): adrenal fatigue (rare, but problematic if true)

Medichecks provides a detailed report with interpretation. Read it carefully. If anything is unclear, Google the marker or ask your doctor.

## Decision Flowchart: What to Do With Your Results

**Total T > 500 ng/dL, Free T in upper half, SHBG normal, other markers fine**
→ Hormones are not your problem. Investigate other causes for symptoms.

**Total T 350-500 ng/dL, but Free T low due to high SHBG**
→ You have a SHBG problem (possibly insulin resistance, liver issues, or genetics). Address insulin sensitivity: reduce refined carbs, train regularly, lose body fat if overweight. Retest in 3 months.

**Total T 250-350 ng/dL, Free T proportionally low, LH and FSH normal**
→ Secondary hypogonadism or just low production. Lifestyle optimization is worth trying: sleep, training, stress management, supplementation. Retest in 12 weeks. If no improvement, consider TRT.

**Total T < 250 ng/dL, High LH and FSH**
→ Primary hypogonadism (testicular problem). TRT is indicated. Consult a doctor or hormone specialist.

**Oestradiol > 50 pg/mL**
→ Possible aromatization issue (overweight? training poorly? too much cardio?). Optimize diet, lose body fat, train with heavy weights. Retest in 3 months.

**Oestradiol < 15 pg/mL**
→ Low—could be from training too hard, too little body fat, genetics, or from AI use (if on TRT). Address: ensure adequate carbs, ease training intensity, check if on AI (and if so, lower dose or stop).

**Cortisol very high (>25 mcg/dL)**
→ Chronic stress. Prioritize stress management. Sleep optimization might help. Retest in 6 weeks. If still high, see doctor to rule out Cushing's (rare) or adrenal issues.

**TSH > 4 or Free T4 low**
→ Possible hypothyroidism. See your GP for thyroid assessment and possible supplementation.

**Vitamin D < 30 ng/mL**
→ Deficient. Supplement D3 4000 IU daily. Retest in 3 months.

**Vitamin D 30-50 ng/mL**
→ Insufficient. Supplement D3 2000-3000 IU daily. Retest in 3 months. Optimal is 50-100.

**Prolactin elevated**
→ Causes sexual dysfunction and mood issues. See a doctor to rule out pituitary tumour (usually benign) and determine cause.

**Ashwagandha and Stress/Testosterone**
- Pratte et al. (2014). "An Alternative Treatment for Anxiety: A Randomized Controlled Trial of Ashwagandha." Journal of Alternative and Complementary Medicine. KSM-66 ashwagandha reduced cortisol and anxiety in 60 days.
- Lopresti et al. (2019). "An Investigation into the Stress-Relieving and Pharmacological Actions of an Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) Extract." Medicine (Baltimore). Shows ashwagandha increases testosterone in men with chronic stress.

**Creatine and Muscle/Performance**
- Kreider et al. (2017). "International Society of Sports Nutrition Position Stand: Safety and Efficacy of Creatine Supplementation." Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition. Creatine monohydrate is safe and effective for strength and muscle retention.

**Zinc and Testosterone**
- Prasad et al. (2008). "Zinc Status and Serum Testosterone Levels." Journal of Nutrition. Zinc deficiency suppresses testosterone; supplementation in deficient individuals restores it.

**Alcohol and Testosterone**
- Sarkola et al. (2000). "Acute Alcohol Consumption Decreases Testosterone Serum Levels." Clinical Physiology and Functional Imaging. Alcohol acutely suppresses testosterone; chronic excessive drinking chronically lowers it.

**Obesity, Insulin Resistance, and Testosterone**
- Bhasin et al. (2007). "Relationship of Fat and Muscle Mass to Bone Density and Serum Testosterone Levels." Journal of Clinical Endocrinology and Metabolism. Obesity increases SHBG and aromatization, lowering free testosterone.

**TRT Safety**
- Bhasin et al. (2010). "Testosterone Therapy in Men with Androgen Deficiency Syndromes." Journal of Clinical Endocrinology and Metabolism. Evidence-based guidelines for TRT dosing and monitoring.
- Sharma et al. (2015). "Cardiovascular Risk in Testosterone Therapy." Current Opinion in Endocrinology. Balanced review of TRT cardiovascular risk (real but manageable with monitoring).

**Oestradiol in Men**
- Jones et al. (2019). "Serum Oestradiol and Mortality in Older Men." New England Journal of Medicine. Too-low oestradiol increases cardiovascular and mortality risk in men.

**Tongkat Ali (Eurycoma longifolia)**
- Henkel et al. (2014). "Clinical Efficacy of Eurycoma longifolia (Tongkat Ali) on Erectile Function and Cardiovascular Markers." Phytomedicine. Tongkat ali extract increased testosterone and improved sexual function in men with low-normal testosterone.

**HCG and Fertility**
- Papadimas et al. (2009). "HCG as Adjunct Treatment to Testosterone Replacement Therapy." Journal of Andrology. HCG preserves spermatogenesis and endogenous testosterone production during TRT.

**Enclomiphene Efficacy**
- Tambi et al. (2012). "Randomized Double-Blind Placebo-Controlled Study on the Effect of Enclomiphene Citrate (CC) on Testosterone Levels in Hypogonadal Men." Journal of Andrology. Enclomiphene (clomiphene's active isomer) increases LH and testosterone in hypogonadal men.

**Exercise Frequency and Hormones**
- Helms et al. (2014). "Evidence-Based Recommendations for Natural Bodybuilding Contest Preparation." Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition. Moderate frequency (3-5 sessions per week) optimizes hormonal response.

**Cardiovascular Health and Testosterone**
- Tivesten et al. (2015). "Endogenous Testosterone and Cardiovascular Outcomes." Current Opinion in Cardiology. Testosterone in physiological range is protective; extreme levels increase risk.

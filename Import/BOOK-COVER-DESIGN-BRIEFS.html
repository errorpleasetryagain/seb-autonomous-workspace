Book Cover Design Briefs

Individual design direction for each of the 9 books. Each cover designed for its specific audience, not a generic template.

What the Research Says

72% of people choose books based on cover alone. At thumbnail size (150px), only bold title + strong colour contrast matters. Everything else disappears.

What sells in 2026: Bold minimalism, strong typography, high contrast, strategic colour. The trend is AWAY from busy, cluttered covers and TOWARD clean, confident design with one clear focal point.

What screams AI/amateur: Stock photo + gradient overlay, overcrowded text (title + subtitle + tagline + credentials all crammed in), ALL CAPS everything, low-res images, genre-mismatched colours.

Your current covers: The dark minimalist style with geometric graphics is actually well-designed and professional looking. The problem is they all look the same. Each book needs its own identity.

Rules for ALL covers:

1. Author name: SEBASTIAN (not Adam J. Turton)

2. Title must be legible at thumbnail size (150px wide)

3. No em dashes in subtitles

4. No ALL CAPS author names (that's an AI book signal)

5. Keep the series numbering (01, 02, etc) as it signals a real catalogue

6. Each cover gets its own colour palette and mood

01 - TRT in the UK

Audience: Men 35-55 considering or already on TRT. Educated, cautious, wants credible information. UK.

Mood: Clinical authority meets accessibility. Think high-end private clinic brochure. Trustworthy, not intimidating.

Colour palette:

Navy
Medical Blue
Off-White
Gold accent

Typography: Clean sans-serif (like Inter or DM Sans). Title bold, subtitle lighter weight. Professional but warm.

Visual concept: Subtle molecular or chemical structure graphic. Or a clean upward arrow/chart motif suggesting improvement. NOT a photo of a syringe or a shirtless man.

Reference vibe: Feels like a book you'd see in a Harley Street clinic waiting room.

02 - Eat for Testosterone

Audience: Men 40+ who want to optimise nutrition for hormones. Interested in food science, not gym bro culture.

Mood: Fresh, natural, appetising. Think premium health food brand crossed with a proper cookbook.

Colour palette:

Forest Green
Warm Orange
Cream
Dark Brown

Typography: Mix of a strong serif for the title ("Eat for") and a bold sans-serif for "Testosterone". Feels premium but approachable.

Visual concept: Clean food-related graphic. Perhaps a minimalist plate arrangement, or stylised food icons (avocado, egg, steak) arranged geometrically. NOT a generic food photo.

Reference vibe: Looks like it belongs next to an Ottolenghi cookbook, not a supplement ad.

03 - The UK Remote Work Pivot

Audience: People 30-50 in traditional industries wanting to switch to remote work. Often women, often in public sector/retail/teaching. Not tech-savvy.

Mood: Optimistic, modern, accessible. Not corporate. Think "your mate who successfully made the switch is telling you how."

Colour palette:

Deep Purple
Amber/Gold
White
Dark Slate

Typography: Friendly sans-serif. Not too corporate. The word "PIVOT" should be the visual anchor, larger and bolder than the rest.

Visual concept: Abstract upward trajectory (your current ascending dots concept is good but needs colour). Or a laptop/location pin motif. Something that says "freedom and possibility" not "another LinkedIn post."

Reference vibe: Feels like a TED Talk companion book.

04 - Eating on GLP-1

Audience: People on Ozempic/Wegovy/Mounjaro, mostly women 35-60, often confused about what and how to eat on medication. UK.

Mood: Pharmaceutical meets approachable. Clean, medical authority, but not cold. Trustworthy. This is the one you said should look "pharmaceutical but approachable" and I agree.

Colour palette:

Teal/Mint
Dark Navy
White
Pale Mint

Typography: Clean, modern sans-serif. Almost medical packaging typography. Title should feel like a trusted NHS leaflet, not a fad diet book.

Visual concept: Minimalist pill/capsule graphic combined with a food element (fork, leaf). Or a clean molecular structure. The crosshair/scope graphic you have now is too militaristic for this audience.

Reference vibe: Looks like it could be sold in Boots next to the Ozempic counter.

IMPORTANT: The GLP-1 market on Amazon is FLOODED with AI-generated books right now (ALL CAPS titles, generic covers). Your cover needs to look obviously different and higher quality to stand out. This is your biggest competitive advantage.

05 - Optimal At 40

Audience: Men turning 40 who want to optimise everything. Successful, tasteful, knows what he wants. Not a gym bro, not a biohacker stereotype. Think: operations manager, senior professional, has his life together but wants to feel better.

Mood: This is the flagship. Premium, confident, sophisticated. You said "extremely tasteful and modern" and that's exactly right. This should look like a book a man picks up in Waterstones, not on a supplement website.

Colour palette:

Charcoal
Gold
Warm Cream
Near Black

Typography: Elegant serif for "Optimal", modern sans-serif for "At 40". Premium book feel. Think the typography on a Johnnie Walker label or an Aston Martin brochure.

Visual concept: Abstract "40" integrated into the design, or a clean geometric figure suggesting peak form. Minimal. Let the typography do the heavy lifting. Gold accents suggest quality without being gaudy.

Reference vibe: Looks like something Waterstones would put on their "Smart Thinking" table.

06 - Aesthetic Hypertrophy Guide

Audience: Men 18-35 who want to look good. Gym culture. Follow influencers like Sam Sulek, CBum, Mike Thurston. Visually driven.

Mood: Bold, energetic, aspirational. This one can be louder than the others. Think gym brand aesthetic (Gymshark, YoungLA).

Colour palette:

Bold Red
Black
White
Dark Grey

Typography: Heavy, bold sans-serif. Condensed. Athletic energy. Think Nike campaign typography.

Visual concept: Abstract muscle fibre graphic, or geometric body form. Bold and graphic. This is the one that can push the design furthest from "book" toward "brand."

Reference vibe: Looks like a Gymshark training guide you'd actually pay for.

07 - Anabolic Breakdown

Audience: Men interested in performance compounds. Knowledgeable, want clinical detail. This is the most niche audience. Not beginners.

Mood: Very pharmaceutical. Clinical. Authoritative. You said this should look "very pharmaceutical" and I agree completely. This is the textbook of the collection.

Colour palette:

Dark Blue-Grey
Purple
Lab White
Steel Grey

Typography: Clean, clinical sans-serif. Almost like pharmaceutical packaging or a medical journal. Mono-weight.

Visual concept: Hexagonal molecular structures (you already have this, it works). Chemical compound diagrams. This should look like a reference book, not a lifestyle guide.

Reference vibe: Looks like a pharmacology textbook you'd trust. BNF meets modern design.

08 - Performance Pharmaceuticals

Audience: Similar to Anabolic Breakdown but broader. People interested in peptides, nootropics, clinical protocols. The "Huberman listener who wants to go deeper."

Mood: Pharmaceutical but with more warmth than Anabolic Breakdown. Evidence-based authority. Less textbook, more "informed guide."

Colour palette:

Burnt Orange
Black
White
Warm Grey

Typography: Bold sans-serif title. Clinical but confident. The bar chart graphic you have now is decent but needs colour.

Visual concept: Data visualisation aesthetic. Bar charts, dose-response curves, or compound comparison graphics. Feels like an infographic made into a book.

Reference vibe: Looks like a Huberman Lab episode companion guide.

09 - AI at Work

Audience: UK professionals in non-tech roles who want to use AI practically. Not developers. Think: HR managers, teachers, operations teams, NHS admin.

Mood: Modern, approachable, practical. Not futuristic sci-fi. Not "robots are coming." More "here's how to actually use this stuff at your desk tomorrow."

Colour palette:

Bright Blue
Dark Slate
White
Green accent

Typography: Friendly, modern sans-serif. Approachable. Not overly corporate.

Visual concept: Simple AI-related icon or circuit pattern, but warm not cold. Maybe a cursor/chat bubble motif. The current grid/circuit design is decent but too generic. Needs to feel specifically "practical workplace AI" not "generic tech."

Reference vibe: Looks like a book Microsoft would give to new employees. Practical, friendly, not intimidating.

NOTE: Consider renaming this. "AI at Work" is extremely generic and competing with hundreds of similar titles. Something like "The UK AI Playbook" or "AI for Your Actual Job" would stand out more.

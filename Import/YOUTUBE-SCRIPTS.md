So I'm 40 years old. I'm not old by any standard, but I was feeling it. And I couldn't figure out why.

For the last couple of years, something had shifted. My energy had dropped. Not dramatically—I could still function, go to the gym, work—but there was this underlying fatigue that wouldn't go away. I'd wake up at 6am feeling like I'd been hit by a truck. By 2pm, I'd need to sit down. I was sleeping 8–9 hours and still felt shattered.

My mood had changed too. I wasn't depressed, exactly. I was just... flat. Things that used to interest me didn't anymore. Work felt heavier. Training felt like a chore instead of something I wanted to do.

The brain fog was worse. I couldn't hold complex thoughts. I'd forget words mid-sentence. Simple decisions took forever. I'd read the same paragraph three times and still not absorb it.

I wrote it off. Getting older, right? Less testosterone, less energy. That's just what happens.

But then I was listening to a podcast—one of those health and optimisation conversations—and the guest mentioned something that stuck with me: "Most men never get their testosterone tested. They assume the fatigue is just age. It's not."

That bothered me. I'd spent my whole life obsessing about optimising things: training, nutrition, sleep. Why would I ignore my hormone levels?

So I booked a blood test. Not with my GP—I had a feeling that would be a frustration. I used Medichecks, one of the UK private testing companies. It's straightforward: order online, book an appointment at a local clinic, give blood, results in a week.

The test cost £35 for basic testosterone. I paid for a fuller panel: testosterone, free testosterone, LH, FSH, prolactin, oestradiol. Total came to about £100.

Honestly, I didn't expect anything unusual. I thought I was just tired because I'm 40 and that's what 40 looks like.

The results came back via email on a Tuesday morning.

*(Pause for effect)*

My total testosterone was 11.2 nmol/L. For reference, normal range for men is typically 8–30 nmol/L. So technically, I was "normal."

But here's what made me sit down: I was at the very bottom of normal. Below 12 is considered "borderline low" by most standards. My free testosterone—the stuff that actually matters, the active hormone—was 180 pmol/L. Most men my age are 250+.

I wasn't normal. I was low-normal, drifting toward deficiency.

And suddenly, everything made sense. The fatigue, the lack of drive, the difficulty thinking clearly—these are textbook low testosterone symptoms.

I sent the results to my GP for context. I wasn't asking for anything specific, just wanted to understand what this meant clinically.

The response: "Your testosterone is within normal range. No action needed."

Which, technically, is true. Normal range is wide, and I was inside it. But I wasn't healthy in that range. I was experiencing symptoms.

This is where the system fails a lot of men. The normal range is so broad that you can be profoundly symptomatic and still be told you're fine.

Here's what I learned over the next few weeks of research:

**First: What these numbers mean.**

Total testosterone is the full amount circulating in your blood. But not all of it is available to your cells. Some of it is bound to proteins (SHBG and albumin). Free testosterone is the unbound stuff—the hormone that actually works.

You can have a total testosterone of 15 nmol/L and feel terrible. Or 20 nmol/L and feel fine, depending on how much is free versus bound.

My free testosterone percentage was about 1.6%. It should be closer to 2.5–3%. So I was losing a lot to binding.

**Second: Why low testosterone happens at 40.**

Testosterone naturally declines with age. After about 30, men lose roughly 1% of testosterone annually. It's not dramatic, but it compounds.

Stress accelerates this. Elevated cortisol suppresses testosterone production. I'd had a stressful two years—business stuff, personal stuff. That probably contributed.

Overtraining without adequate recovery can suppress testosterone. I'd been grinding hard in the gym without a planned deload in years. That definitely contributed.

Sleep issues suppress testosterone. I'd been sleeping 7–8 hours but not sleeping *well*. Fragmented, waking multiple times. That's worse than sleeping 6 hours straight.

Body composition: carrying excess body fat increases aromatisation—conversion of testosterone to oestrogen. I was about 18% body fat, which isn't terrible, but it was probably part of the picture.

**Third: What I did about it.**

I didn't immediately jump to TRT (testosterone replacement therapy). I wanted to try to recover naturally first.

The evidence suggests that some of this might be recoverable if you address the root causes.

So:

**Sleep**: I became obsessive about sleep. I stopped working after 8pm. I cut out screens an hour before bed. I kept my bedroom cold (around 16–17 degrees Celsius). I started tracking sleep with an Oura Ring to see the difference. Within two weeks, my sleep quality improved. I went from 70–75% deep sleep to 85–90%.

**Stress management**: I took two weeks completely off work. Just off. No emails, no calls. I did some training, a lot of reading, a lot of walking. The point was to let my cortisol drop and my nervous system reset. It worked. I felt different even after a week.

**Training**: I cut my volume by 40%. Instead of six sessions a week, I did four. Instead of 15–18 sets per muscle, I did 10–12. But I kept the intensity high—still lifting heavy. The idea was to maintain stimulus while reducing recovery demands.

**Body composition**: I went on a very modest cut. Not aggressive—just 200 calories below maintenance, focusing on losing fat. Dropped from 18% to 15% body fat over 12 weeks. That was enough to move the needle on hormone balance.

**Supplementation**: I added a few things that have some evidence for supporting testosterone naturally. Vitamin D (most men are deficient and it affects testosterone production)—I got tested and was at 45 nmol/L, which is low. I supplemented to get to 100+. Magnesium (supports sleep and testosterone). Zinc (necessary for testosterone production, but only if deficient—I wasn't, but supplemented slightly anyway for recovery).

**Diet**: I added more saturated fat. Testosterone is a steroid hormone made from cholesterol. If you're eating zero fat, you're limiting your raw materials. I went from roughly 20% of calories from fat to 30%. Most from whole sources: meat, eggs, dairy.

**Retesting**: After three months of these changes, I retested.

*(Another pause)*

Total testosterone: 14.8 nmol/L. Free testosterone: 240 pmol/L.

Not massive changes, but meaningful. I'd moved from "bottom of normal, borderline low" to "healthy mid-range."

And—this is what really convinced me it worked—my symptoms had mostly resolved. I had energy again. My mood had lifted. I could think clearly. My motivation for training had returned.

**What I learned from all this:**

First, your GP is not your performance coach. Their job is to determine if you're in the disease range. If you're not in the disease range, they're technically done. But optimal health and disease absence are different things. You can be non-diseased and still feel terrible.

Second, symptoms matter. If you feel like shit and your labs are in the "normal" range, don't dismiss it. The normal range is a population average. It doesn't mean you feel good in that range.

Third, a lot of how you feel is modifiable. Sleep, stress, training, body composition, nutrition—these all matter for hormone production. You can't out-supplement bad foundations.

Fourth, private testing exists for a reason. It's quicker, you get more detail, and you can actually act on the results. The NHS testing is fine if you're hunting for disease. For optimisation, private testing is worth the £100.

And finally: worth getting tested if you're male, over 35, and experiencing unexplained fatigue, mood changes, or brain fog. It's 30 minutes of your time and £35. The information is worth the investment.

I didn't end up needing TRT. But I'm genuinely grateful I got tested. Because I could have spent the next five years thinking I was just getting old, when actually I had a solvable problem.

*(Direct to camera)*

That's the honest version of this. Not a miracle story, not a tragedy story. Just what testosterone testing revealed about why I was feeling the way I was feeling.

### SCRIPT (1,900 words of spoken content)

---

**The NHS testosterone system explained.**

The NHS has guidelines for testosterone treatment. They're clear and evidence-based. But they're conservative. By design.

Here's how it works:

Your GP tests your testosterone. They get a result. They compare it to the normal range for a 40-year-old man, which is roughly 8–30 nmol/L.

If you're above 8, technically you're normal. Your GP is done with the conversation.

If you're between 8–12 (what people call "borderline low"), your GP might have a conversation. But they're unlikely to treat. Because the NHS guidance says: "Testosterone replacement is indicated for persistent symptoms AND consistently low levels."

The catch: "Consistently low" usually means you need two tests, both below 8 nmol/L, both on separate occasions. Most GPs won't even run a second test unless you push hard.

And "persistent symptoms"—well, that's subjective. Fatigue, mood, low libido, erectile dysfunction. Your GP might attribute these to depression, stress, or age, rather than hormones.

So the system filters out a lot of men. Men who have genuine symptoms and sub-optimal hormone levels, but aren't at the threshold for NHS intervention.

**Why are the guidelines so conservative?**

Several reasons.

First: testosterone replacement is a controlled medication. It's not without side effects or risks. The NHS takes a "do no harm" approach. If you're technically in the normal range, the risk-benefit calculation doesn't favour treatment.

Second: NHS resources are limited. Testosterone replacement, once started, requires ongoing monitoring. Blood tests every 3–6 months. GP appointments. They can't treat every man with vague fatigue who might have hormonal issues.

Third: the evidence for treating "borderline low" testosterone is genuinely mixed. Some studies show benefit in replacing testosterone in men with levels of 8–10 nmol/L. Others don't. So there's intellectual honesty in the caution.

Fourth: there's liability concern. If a GP prescribes testosterone to a man with a heart condition or prostate issue, and something goes wrong, that's a liability issue. Safer to avoid it.

**The problem with this approach.**

The NHS system works if you're either definitely normal (30+ nmol/L) or definitely low (below 8 nmol/L). But there's a big middle ground.

A man with 10 nmol/L is technically treatable. A man with 12 nmol/L is not. But physiologically, they're in the same situation. Both have sub-optimal hormone levels. Both might be symptomatic.

The man at 12 nmol/L is told "you're fine." But he doesn't feel fine. He's fatigued, unmotivated, struggling. His GP has shrugged and said "this isn't a hormone problem, this is age or depression or stress."

And maybe it is. But maybe it's not. Maybe his testosterone is genuinely contributing.

The NHS system doesn't account for this nuance.

**What "borderline low" actually means.**

Here's the terminology problem.

Doctors and blood tests use "normal range." This is a population reference. It's the range that includes 95% of healthy men. By definition, 5% of healthy men fall outside it.

But within the normal range, there's a spectrum. Some men feel great at 10 nmol/L. Most men feel terrible. Why the difference? Genetic variation, baseline hormones at 20 years old, individual sensitivity.

"Borderline low" is a term that means: you're in the normal range, but at the low end, and your symptoms suggest you might benefit from a higher level.

It's not a disease diagnosis. It's a clinical judgement call. And the NHS doesn't really make those calls—they stick to clear thresholds.

Private clinics, by contrast, will treat "borderline low" if you're symptomatic. Because their risk calculation is different and their patient population is self-selected (people paying for this care tend to be motivated).

**What your options are if your GP won't help.**

Option 1: Private clinic via the NHS.

Some NHS regions have private TRT clinics within the system. You can be referred by your GP. This is rare and hard to access, but if you can swing it, it's the fastest route to treatment within NHS care.

Most men can't access this because it depends on their region and their GP's willingness to refer.

Option 2: Private consultation with a private doctor.

You pay out-of-pocket to see a private doctor—often £150–300 per consultation—who can prescribe testosterone privately.

This is faster (you'll be treated in one or two visits rather than months of GP appointments), more flexible (private doctors are less bound by NHS guidelines), and gives you more control.

The catch: you're paying privately. And you need ongoing monitoring, which means more private visits.

Rough cost: Initial consultation £200, blood tests £100–150, testosterone treatment £30–60 per month, follow-up appointments £150 every 3–6 months.

So you're looking at £1,000–1,500 annually for treatment and monitoring.

Option 3: Private TRT clinics.

These are online-based clinics that specialise in testosterone replacement. Examples in the UK: Optimale, Leger, Balance My Hormones.

You do an initial consultation online, provide blood test results (or they arrange testing), and if you're approved, they prescribe and post testosterone to you monthly.

Cost: Usually £30–60 monthly for treatment, plus consultation fees (£100–200 per visit).

The advantage: streamlined, relatively affordable, designed specifically for TRT.

The disadvantage: less personalised, less integration with your overall health (they're focused on testosterone specifically), and you need to find a GP willing to do follow-up blood work (some will, some won't).

Option 4: Do nothing and manage foundations.

Many men choose to address sleep, stress, training, body composition, and nutrition without pharmaceutical intervention. If your testosterone is 10–15 nmol/L and you're symptomatic, optimising these factors might push you into a healthier range.

This takes longer (3–6 months to see meaningful changes) but it's free and addresses root causes.

This is my advice to most men as a first step: optimise foundations. If that doesn't work and you're still symptomatic, then consider treatment.

**The private TRT clinic landscape in the UK (2026).**

There are now several TRT clinics operating in the UK. Here's what they typically offer:

**Optimale**
- Consultation fee: £200 initial, £99 follow-ups
- Treatment: £35–50/month depending on dose
- Offering testosterone therapy, LH/FSH, sometimes oestrogen management
- Fast turnaround (usually treated within 1–2 weeks if approved)

**Leger Health**
- Consultation: £195 initial
- Treatment: £40–60/month
- Online-based, GP integration sometimes possible
- Good customer service, straightforward process

**Balance My Hormones**
- Consultation: £150–200
- Treatment: £35–45/month
- Personalised approach, willing to discuss non-TRT options
- Slower approval process (2–4 weeks) but more thorough

**TestoBoost, Gameday, others**
- Various pricing models, mostly similar £30–60/month treatment costs
- Quality varies; read reviews

These are all legitimate, operating within legal bounds, and staffed by real doctors. But choose carefully. Read recent reviews, check if they're registered with appropriate medical bodies, and see if they'll do follow-up blood work.

**The red flags with private TRT.**

Some dodgy operators exist. Red flags:

- They'll prescribe without baseline blood work
- They promise results in days
- They offer unlimited testosterone (responsible prescribers adjust doses)
- They have no follow-up monitoring
- They're substantially cheaper (£10/month testosterone is suspiciously cheap)

Legitimate clinics will: request baseline blood work, explain the risks, start conservative doses, retest regularly (every 3–6 months), and adjust based on symptoms and bloodwork.

**What's actually the best approach?**

Honest answer: depends on your situation.

If your testosterone is clearly below 8 nmol/L and you're very symptomatic, push your GP to refer you to a specialist. The NHS system does eventually treat, if you're patient enough.

If your testosterone is 8–12 nmol/L and you're somewhat symptomatic, try optimising foundations for 3 months. Sleep, stress, training, body composition, nutrition. Retest. Often you'll see movement.

If you're still symptomatic after that, go private. Pick a reputable clinic, do the consultation, and if you're approved, start treatment. You'll know within 4–6 weeks if it's helping (energy, mood, libido, strength all improve or don't).

If your testosterone is clearly normal (15+) and you're symptomatic, the problem is probably not testosterone. Could be thyroid, depression, sleep, stress. Investigate those instead.

**The future: normalising this conversation.**

What's frustrating about the current system is that testosterone is a health marker. Like blood pressure, cholesterol, or vitamin D. You should be able to test it routinely and treat it if you're symptomatic.

The NHS is cautious (understandably). But this caution leaves a lot of men suffering unnecessarily.

Ideally, every man would get basic hormone testing at 40 as a routine health screen. If you're low and symptomatic, there'd be a clear pathway to treatment. Just like with blood pressure.

We're not there yet. So for now, you navigate the system as it exists: try your GP, get referred if possible, go private if necessary.

It's not ideal, but it works.

If your GP has told you your testosterone is fine and you feel terrible, you're not crazy. Your GP is following guidelines. But that doesn't mean there isn't a solution. Investigate further. Get a second opinion. Consider private testing if needed. You deserve to feel good.

**What is the Huberman morning protocol?**

Andrew Huberman is a neuroscientist at Stanford. He's become influential in the performance/biohacking space because he's rigorous about citing research.

His morning protocol is: get bright light exposure immediately upon waking, do cold exposure (cold shower or ice bath) shortly after, then do exercise.

The theory: bright light sets your circadian rhythm, increases cortisol (good in the morning, bad at night), and improves alertness. Cold exposure activates the sympathetic nervous system, increases noradrenaline, and improves focus and mood. Exercise amplifies all of this.

In combination, the idea is: you're maximally alert, hormonally optimised, and ready to tackle the day. And by extension, your sleep that night improves because your circadian rhythm is locked in.

It's sold as a biological hack: 30 minutes in the morning replaces coffee, improves productivity, and optimises your hormones.

Sounds good in theory. Let's see if it works.

**The experiment setup.**

I committed to 30 days. I tracked sleep and metrics with an Oura Ring (for heart rate variability, deep sleep, REM sleep, body temperature). I kept a daily log of mood, energy, and subjective focus.

I also tested baseline first. One week of my normal routine, then 30 days of the protocol.

My normal routine: wake naturally (usually 6:30am), coffee first thing, gym around 9am, normal shower.

The protocol:

1. **Light exposure** (immediately upon waking): 5–10 minutes of bright light. I used a 10,000 lux light box in winter. In warmer months, this would be 10 minutes of morning sunlight outside.

2. **Cold exposure** (15 minutes after waking): 3–5 minutes of cold water immersion. I used a cold shower first (started at 12°C, then dialled it down). Most days I did 2–3 minutes cold shower.

3. **Exercise** (within 30 minutes of cold exposure): 10–20 minutes of moderate-to-intense exercise. I did a quick circuit: rowing, burpees, kettlebell swings. Not a full gym session—just something to elevate heart rate.

All of this done before 8am. Then normal breakfast and the day.

**What I measured.**

Oura Ring gave me:
- Total sleep, deep sleep, REM sleep percentages
- Heart rate variability (HRV, a measure of nervous system recovery)
- Resting heart rate
- Body temperature during sleep

Subjective metrics (daily log):
- Energy level (1–10)
- Mood (1–10)
- Focus/clarity (1–10)
- Afternoon crash (yes/no, and severity)
- How I felt about the routine (sustainable or miserable)

I also tracked workout performance (rough strength metrics) to see if there were training differences.

**Baseline results (week 1).**

Before the protocol:

- Average sleep: 7 hours 15 minutes (range: 6h45m to 8h)
- Deep sleep: 18% of total
- REM sleep: 22% of total
- HRV: 45 (pretty average for my age)
- Resting heart rate: 58 bpm
- Energy: 6.5/10 average (dropped to 5/10 by afternoon)
- Mood: 6.5/10
- Focus: 6/10, usually drops by 2pm
- Afternoon crash: yes, usually around 2–3pm, mild but noticeable

So baseline: I was sleeping okay, feeling okay, but had a clear afternoon energy drop.

**Weeks 2–4: The protocol.**

First three days were brutal.

Cold exposure is horrible when you're not used to it. Standing under a 12°C shower for 2 minutes feels like punishment. Your body is screaming. Your breath stops momentarily. Then you get out and you're... actually alert.

The energy from the cold + exercise combination is real. Within 20 minutes, I felt genuinely more awake than I ever did with coffee.

But it's a commitment. Every single morning, 30 minutes of deliberate effort before anything else.

Days 4–10: things settled. My body adapted to the cold. It stopped feeling like torture and became just uncomfortable. Not fun, but tolerable.

By day 8, I noticed something: I wasn't having the usual afternoon crash. I'd feel tired around 4pm, but it wasn't the "hit a wall" crash of before. More like a gradual shift.

Energy levels were better. Not dramatically—maybe 7.5/10 instead of 6.5/10—but consistent.

Sleep was weird. Some nights I slept great (7h 45m, 22% deep sleep, 25% REM). Other nights it was worse (6h 30m, 16% deep sleep). No clear pattern yet.

Days 11–20: established routine. My body expected the cold. Adaptation was complete.

The data got interesting here:

- Sleep: increased slightly to 7h 30m average (up from 7h 15m)
- Deep sleep: increased to 20% (up from 18%)
- REM sleep: stable, 22%
- HRV: increased to 52 (up from 45)
- Energy: consistently 7–8/10, afternoon energy held better
- Mood: improved noticeably. Not dramatically, but more stable baseline
- Focus: better. I could work for 3–4 hours without the usual drift

Subjectively, this is the sweet spot. The routine wasn't exhausting, it was habit. And I felt noticeably better.

Days 21–30: fine-tuning.

By this point, I was curious whether I could dial down the intensity. What if I skipped the cold exposure one day? What if I did light exposure without the full protocol?

Skipped cold exposure on day 23: next day's sleep was worse (slept 40 minutes less). HRV dropped. So cold exposure genuinely seemed to matter.

Skipped exercise on day 27: next day energy was lower. Afternoon crash came back (mildly).

Reduced light exposure to 5 minutes (instead of 10) on day 25: no obvious difference, so probably 5–10 minutes is the window, not more.

**Final results after 30 days.**

Comparing baseline week to final week:

- Sleep: +15 minutes per night average
- Deep sleep: +2% (from 18% to 20%)
- REM sleep: stable, 22%
- HRV: +7 (from 45 to 52)

- Long-term safety is debated in literature, but therapeutic TRT in healthy men is generally considered safe

**The honest take:**
TRT is worth doing if you're genuinely low and symptomatic. But only if you're committed to:
- Treating it seriously long-term
- Doing regular blood work
- Adjusting protocol based on results
- Doing the foundational work (sleep, stress, training, diet) alongside it
